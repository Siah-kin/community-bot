You showed up early. The room moved on. Your standing did not come with you.

You debugged the thing nobody else would touch. You kept the chat civil at 3am. You filed the bug before it cost anyone money. Then the wave broke, the founder went quiet, and the room emptied out.

Your standing went with it. The OG role, the respect, the history of showing up first. It lived in one Discord that is now a graveyard, and it died there.

You still carry it in your head. You know what you built and where. Nobody else can check it, and nothing pays it forward.

The work was real. The record was not.

Every program built to fix this got farmed.

If you have run one, you know how it ends. You open a contributor campaign, a bug bounty, an ambassador track. Week one is real. Week three is people gaming the number you were willing to publish. By week six you are paying for noise, and you cannot tell the bouncer from the leech.

The rot is always the same. The reward chased a number, and a number is a thing people can manufacture. Count the messages, you get message spam. Count the reports, you get junk reports. The metric becomes the target, and the target stops telling the truth.

So the scepticism you walked in with is correct. The fix is not a cleverer number.

The fix is judging the work on whether it actually held.

You already trust a reputation system. It is the one in your head.

You know who pays, who ships, who lies. You trust it because you earned every entry yourself.

So the real question is not whether portable credit is a good idea. Everyone wants their work to follow them. The question is harder. Can a credit somebody else records ever feel as solid as the one you carry yourself?

We do not get to answer that for you. That is what this alpha is for. You run the loop, you look at the record, and you decide if it holds up against the track record you already trust.

If it does not beat what is in your head, it failed. That is the bar.

We are testing the credit, not the buttons.

A person confirms it. Then it is on record.

Here the call is made by a human, not a counter. Someone with standing in the room looks at what you sent and decides whether it was real. Not whether it is popular. Not whether it got upvotes. Whether the bug was a bug, whether the fix held, whether the thing you reported was worth reporting.

That is the whole difference, and it is a small one on purpose. A vote can be swarmed. A like can be bought. A person who puts their name on the confirmation, and who gets judged later on whether they called it right, has every reason to call it straight.

When they confirm it, the system writes it down. A ticket number, the confirmation, the outcome. That record is yours to point at, and it does not live or die with this room.

Informal reputation you cannot show is worth less than a plain record you can.

One path. You take the first step.

One way to make a contribution that leaves a record. It is small on purpose. You can run the first step in a minute and watch it work. The steps below carry an honest tag for what is proven today and what we are still checking with the first real users.

1

You file it. Live now
Open /ticket in the bot and write a real bug: what you did, what you expected, what broke. A screenshot or link helps. You get a number and a time back. This step works today. Test it yourself.

2

It becomes a case. Checking now
If other people hit the same thing, the reports join up, so one fix can answer many. You are not shouting into a channel that scrolls away.

3

A person confirms it. Checking now
A human, not an algorithm, reads it and decides if it is a real bug worth crediting. Judged on whether it held up, not on how loud it was.

4

Credit, then a receipt. Checking now
Your contribution credit is recorded, and a receipt closes the loop: the ticket number, the confirmation, the outcome. Something you point to later, not a promise you take on faith.

Step one is live today. The rest is built and running in production. The last mile, the receipt and credit reaching you, is what we are checking right now with the first real users. The status section below says exactly where each part stands.

What is live, what we are checking, what is not yet.

Plain status. Nothing here is called live unless it already leaves a record you can check yourself.

Live now: the /ticket intake. Open /ticket in the bot and send a report. It comes back with a number and a 48h target. Test it today, this minute, without taking our word for it.

Checking now, with the first real users: receipt delivery and the contribution credit. The code is in production. What we are watching is the full path with real people: a real bug confirmed by an admin, the reporter getting their credit, the receipt landing. When that runs clean, this line moves up to live.

Later: thank points (the credit you earn for helping) converting to ETH, the money the network runs on. That rail starts on test ETH, which is practice money on a test network with no real value, so the whole path is proven before any real value moves. There is no automatic reward today.

Not running at all yet: there is no honesty penalty, no locked deposit, no voting weight, and no token reward live today. None of it. When any of those start, this page will say so in these words. If a page tells you they are running before you can point to the record, close the page.

If you run a room, you hold the confirm button.

If you have watched a program get farmed, you are the reader this was built for. The contributor sends the report. You are the one who decides whether it counts. The credit that gets written down is worth exactly what your judgment is worth, and that is the point.

We do not ask you to trust a score. We ask you to make the call you already make in your head every day, the one where you know who is carrying the room and who is farming it. We write that call down so it survives you. When this community is gone, the people who actually held it up have something to carry to the next one.

This is why your scepticism is the asset, not a problem to manage. A confirmation is worth as much as the person giving it is hard to fool. Someone who has watched their own program get farmed is the last person a farmer wants holding the gate.

Where everyone else sees users to bill, the job here is keeping the people who keep the room honest.

Seven years of running rooms, written down this time.

This is not a clever idea from last quarter. It is seven years of managing communities by hand, deciding what counted and what was noise, watching which rewards got farmed and which held. That judgment used to live in one operator's head. Now it is in the code that decides what a confirmation looks like.

That is the part that is hard to copy. Anyone can fork the bot, and you should, it is open. But the calls baked into it came from doing the work, year after year, in rooms that mostly do not exist anymore.

We are not selling you the future of anything. The job is narrow on purpose: wire credit to real work so it stops getting eaten by the next cycle.

You do not have to trust that. Read the code.
