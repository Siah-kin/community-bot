Towards Sustainable Crypto Communities: A Human-Centric Contribution and Reward Mechanism

Working draft: design proposal and evaluation plan, not a results claim. Field-data, pilot-result, and pilot-capability markers show what still needs measured evidence; treasury payout is not live.

Abstract

Most crypto reward systems pay for visible activity: posts, votes, reactions, referral clicks. Activity is cheap to fake, so these systems reward farm-and-dump behaviour and the people who can manufacture engagement at scale. At the same time, the value that does get created flows to capital, to liquidity providers, stakers, and early holders, while the humans who moderate, onboard newcomers, fix documentation, and resolve bugs receive a small residual. This paper describes a two-layer mechanism that tries to close both gaps. An economic base accrues a flat fee per unit of activity into a treasury (not to liquidity providers), with no inflationary emissions and transparent accounting. A contribution layer mints durable, non-transferable, identity-bound credit for work that is judged against outcomes rather than counted as activity, by reviewers whose standing rises and falls with the long-run correctness of their judgements. The treasury then distributes value pro-rata to each contributor's share of credit.

This is not a new theory. It is a careful integration of known primitives: Ostrom's design principles for commons governance, EigenTrust-style reputation, Douceur's Sybil impossibility, peer prediction and the Bayesian Truth Serum for truthful elicitation, retroactive public-goods funding, and Goodhart's law as the problem statement. The contribution of this paper is the integration into a single loop with a minimal pilot of its contribution path, a semi-formal model that states where the incentives hold and where they do not, an honest treatment of the crux (outcome measurement for soft work is itself hard and gameable), and field-grounding claims from the author's stated multi-year operating history in adversarial crypto communities [FIELD DATA: operator to confirm the operating period and its scope]. The pilot is a Telegram community bot running the bug-report intake and contribution-credit path; treasury payout is not yet live. We report no results yet; instead we pre-register a falsifiable evaluation wired to the rows that the pilot is meant to write and verify.

1. Introduction

A crypto community is an adversarial environment. The moment a reward is attached to a measurable signal, some fraction of participants optimise the signal rather than the thing it was meant to stand for. When the signal is activity, posts, votes, reactions, referrals, the optimisation is cheap: activity can be bought, scripted, or Sybil-farmed for far less than the reward it captures. The predictable result is farm-and-dump: a cohort arrives for a reward window, inflates the metric, extracts value, and leaves nothing that outlasts the payout.

The second failure is distributional. Even in communities that genuinely create value, the rail that distributes that value is usually wired to holdings. Liquidity providers earn fees, stakers earn emissions, early holders ride appreciation. The humans who actually keep a community alive, the moderators who absorb conflict, the members who onboard newcomers, the contributors who fix the docs and report the bugs, are paid in gratitude. Capital is rewarded for being present; labour is not.

This paper describes a mechanism that targets both failures at once. It has two layers.

The economic base (Section 4) charges a constant, flat fee per unit of activity. That fee accrues to a treasury rather than to liquidity providers. There are no inflationary emissions, and the treasury's accounting is transparent. The treasury is the funding source for contributor rewards.

The contribution layer (Section 4) is where the anti-gaming work happens. Contributors submit structured claims of completed work with evidence. Reviewers who hold standing, a reputation score reflecting the accuracy of their past judgements, evaluate each claim against an outcome rubric. Validation is outcome-anchored: the question is whether the fix held or the problem stayed resolved, not how much activity occurred. Accepted work mints durable, non-transferable, non-expiring credit tied to identity, and the treasury periodically distributes value pro-rata to each contributor's share of total credit. Reviewer standing itself rises and falls with the long-run outcomes of the decisions a reviewer made.

We are honest about the crux up front. The whole anti-gaming claim rests on judging outcomes rather than activity, but for soft work, moderation, onboarding, content, support, the outcome is itself hard to measure and, once measured, becomes a new target to game (Goodhart's law). Section 5 confronts this directly: outcome anchoring narrows the attack surface, it does not close it. The honest position of this paper is that the mechanism is incentive-aligned within a stated regime, not incentive-proof, and that it is not trustless: it depends on human review and off-chain signals.

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section 2 locates the work in five literatures. Section 3 gives a semi-formal model with the credit, standing, outcome, and reward functions and an incentive analysis that states where honesty is and is not the better strategy. Section 4 describes the full system: economic base, contribution layer, and governance. Section 5 specifies how outcomes are measured per contribution class and confronts the Goodhart problem. Section 6 is a hardened threat model across six attack classes. Section 7 grounds the design in field observation and describes the minimal pilot. Section 8 pre-registers the evaluation against the pilot's rows. Section 9 states limitations and future work. Section 10 concludes.

2. Related Work

This paper does not introduce a new theory. It integrates established primitives from five literatures: mechanism design, commons governance, reputation systems, truthful elicitation, and public-goods funding. We locate our contribution by stating, for each literature, what it gives us and where it stops short for the problem of rewarding soft human work in an adversarial crypto community.

2.1 Mechanism design and incentive compatibility

The contribution layer is an elicitation problem: we ask reviewers to report private judgements about work quality, and we want truthful reports. The classical mechanism-design program (Hurwicz, Maskin, and Myerson; see Myerson 1991 for the canonical treatment) studies when a designer can structure payoffs so that self-interested agents reveal private information. The revelation principle and the impossibility results around dominant-strategy implementation (Gibbard 1973; Satterthwaite 1975) frame the limits we inherit: with money on the table and colluding agents, no general mechanism is both efficient and fully strategy-proof. We do not claim strategy-proofness. We follow the draft's honest stance and instead bound the gain from deviation under a stated threat model (Section 6), which is the weaker but defensible target. Our standing function is closest in spirit to a scoring rule that pays for accuracy over time rather than for any single report.

2.2 Commons governance: Ostrom's design principles

The governance layer is a direct application of Ostrom's (1990) design principles for long-enduring common-pool resource institutions. Four principles map onto our mechanism almost verbatim:

  • Clearly defined boundaries maps to identity-tied credit and a defined contributor set.
  • Monitoring by accountable members maps to standing-weighted reviewers drawn from the community, not an external authority.
  • Graduated sanctions maps to standing loss scaled to the severity and persistence of a reviewer's errors.
  • Polycentric, nested governance maps to the community setting categories, rubrics, outcome windows, and treasury split, rather than a single central planner.

Ostrom's central empirical finding, that durable institutions are governed by their own monitored-and-sanctioned participants rather than by privatisation or top-down control, is the reason our review layer is human and community-internal rather than purely algorithmic. The cost is that the system is not trustless; we treat that as a deliberate trade, not an oversight.

2.3 Reputation systems

"Standing" is a reputation score. The design space for such scores in open, pseudonymous networks is well charted. Resnick et al. (2000) survey the function and failure modes of online reputation systems (notably the ease of identity whitewashing and the thinness of negative feedback). EigenTrust (Kamvar, Schlosser, and Garcia-Molina 2003) gives the canonical transitive-trust computation: trust flows along edges and is aggregated into a global score, with pre-trusted peers anchoring the system against manipulation. Our standing function differs in two ways that matter for this problem. First, it is outcome-anchored: standing updates on the realised, time-windowed outcome of a judgement, not on peer endorsement, which closes the most direct mutual-rating collusion path. Second, credit (the reward-bearing quantity) is non-transferable and non-expiring, separating the "who decides" graph from the "who gets paid" ledger. EigenTrust-style aggregation remains usable for the reviewer-trust graph; we cite it as the substrate for that sub-problem rather than as the whole solution.

2.4 Sybil resistance

Any identity-tied reward invites Sybil attack. Douceur (2002) established the core impossibility: without a trusted certifying authority or a scarce resource to bind identities, a single adversary can present arbitrarily many distinct identities, and purely local tests cannot reliably distinguish them. This is why our mitigation does not rely on detecting fake accounts after the fact. It relies on the fact that credit is minted only on accepted, outcome-validated work judged by standing-weighted reviewers: fabricating identities is cheap, but fabricating durable accepted contributions that survive an outcome window is not. This shifts the marginal cost of farming onto the work itself; it does not sidestep Douceur's impossibility. An adversary who pays N genuine identities to do N genuine units of work still collects N shares, and at that point the treasury is paying for N real outcomes. We treat that as the intended failure mode (Section 6.1 residual risk), consistent with the proof-of-personhood and cost-of-forgery framings in the broader literature (Siddarth et al. 2020).

2.5 Truthful elicitation and peer prediction

The crux of the paper, judging soft work where the ground truth is not directly observed, is the peer-prediction problem. Miller, Resnick, and Zeckhauser (2005) show that when there is no external verifier, one can still pay raters in a way that makes honest reporting a Nash equilibrium, by scoring each report against its predictive value for others' reports. Prelec's (2004) Bayesian Truth Serum strengthens this by rewarding answers that are surprisingly common, more common than respondents themselves predicted, which elicits truthful reports even for subjective questions, without any objective outcome. These results are the formal backbone for the part of our mechanism the draft previously only gestured at. Where an outcome window exists (a fixed bug, a moderation decision that was not overturned), we anchor on the realised outcome directly. Where it does not (much content and onboarding work), the design intends to fall back to peer-prediction-style scoring of reviewer agreement against predicted agreement; this fallback is specified but not yet deployed (Section 9). We are explicit that this fallback is weaker: it makes truthful reporting an equilibrium, not a dominant strategy, and it is vulnerable to coordinated reviewers, which is exactly the collusion case our standing-decay and appeals layers are designed to make expensive rather than impossible.

2.6 Retroactive and quadratic public-goods funding

The treasury-to-contributor distribution is a public-goods funding problem. Buterin, Hitzig, and Weyl (2019) generalise quadratic voting to funding, where the matching subsidy scales with the number of distinct contributors rather than the capital they commit, directly attacking whale capture. The retroactive variant, pay for outcomes after they are known rather than forecasting them, is the principle behind Optimism's Retro Funding program (Optimism Documentation, n.d.; Optimism Collective 2024) and Gitcoin's quadratic funding deployments (Gitcoin, 2026). Our design takes the retroactive timing (credit and payout follow validated outcomes) but distributes pro-rata to credit share rather than via a quadratic match; we discuss in Section 4 why pro-rata-on-non-transferable-credit is a more Sybil-robust target than quadratic-on-donations for our specific contributor population. The non-transferable, identity-bound credit unit is closest to the Hypercerts model of durable, attributable impact records (Hypercerts Foundation, n.d.).

2.7 Contribution graphs

Translating heterogeneous community work into a single reward-bearing quantity is the problem SourceCred set out to solve, by building a weighted contribution graph and flowing "cred" through it via a PageRank-style computation (SourceCred documentation, n.d.). We share the goal, a durable, auditable record of who did what, but diverge on the scoring substrate: SourceCred weights are activity-derived and therefore exposed to the gaming failure below, whereas our credit is minted only on reviewed, outcome-anchored acceptance. We treat the contribution graph as a record-keeping and provenance layer, not as the value-assignment function.

2.8 Goodhart's law: why activity metrics fail

The unifying critique behind the entire paper is Goodhart's law (Goodhart 1975; sharpened as Strathern's 1997 formulation, "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure"). Crypto reward systems that pay for posts, votes, and referrals are paying for the measure (visible activity), which is cheap to fabricate; the result is farm-and-dump behaviour. Our entire design is an attempt to move the target from the measure (activity) to the thing the measure was a proxy for (durable, validated outcomes). We are candid that this only displaces Goodhart rather than escaping it: once accepted outcome is the target, the gaming pressure moves to the outcome-validation step, which is precisely why the reviewer-collusion and outcome-gaming branches of the threat model carry the most weight.

2.9 Positioning

Taken together, the mechanism is a careful composition: Ostrom for the governance shell, EigenTrust-style reputation for the reviewer graph, Douceur's impossibility for why we bind reward to work rather than to identity, peer prediction and Bayesian Truth Serum for the soft-work elicitation crux, retroactive public-goods funding for the payout timing, contribution graphs for provenance, and Goodhart's law for the problem statement. The contribution of this paper is not any one of these primitives but their integration into a single loop whose bug-report contribution path is being piloted in an adversarial community, with payout still to come (Section 7).

3. A Semi-Formal Model

The prior sections described the mechanism in prose. This section gives it notation so the claims can be inspected and, later, falsified against pilot data. The model is semi-formal on purpose: the soft contributions this system targets (moderation, onboarding, content, support) do not admit clean closed-form outcomes, so some terms below are defined operationally (as a measured signal in [0,1]) rather than derived from first principles. Where that is the case we say so. Nothing here is strategy-proof; the incentive analysis at the end states exactly where it holds and where it does not.

Throughout, the running example is the minimal pilot: a Telegram community bot running a bug-report loop. A user files a report via a /ticket command, which writes a durable row (ticket id, author, timestamp). A reviewer who knows the project judges whether it was a real bug. If confirmed and the fix holds, the contributor earns durable credit and keeps a receipt. Treasury payout is planned on test funds, not yet live. Each formal object below is annotated with its pilot counterpart.

3.1 Notation

Define the actors and objects.

  • Contributors i ∈ C. The person who does the work (files the bug report).
  • Reviewers r ∈ R. The person who judges the work (the admin who confirms the bug). The sets C and R may overlap; a contributor in one submission can be a reviewer in another, subject to the conflict rule below.
  • Submissions x ∈ X. One claim of completed work. Each x carries:
  • a type τ(x) ∈ Τ (e.g. bug_report, moderation, localisation, content),
  • evidence ev(x) (the structured payload: description, logs, links, the ticket row),
  • a contributor i(x),
  • an assigned reviewer set A(x) ⊆ R.
  • Time is discrete, indexed by t. Rewards are paid at epochs e = 1, 2, …, each a fixed wall-clock interval (e.g. monthly).

Three scores drive everything: reviewer standing s_r(t) ∈ [0,1], an outcome signal o(x) ∈ [0,1], and contributor credit c_i ≥ 0. We build them in order.

A note on two outcome glyphs. Two diacritics on o appear below and are easy to conflate, so we fix them once here and use them consistently:

  • ō(x) ∈ [0,1] is the continuous outcome strength (how fully the work held), measured at the close of the window.
  • ô(x) = 1[o(x) ≥ ω_τ] ∈ {0,1} is the resolved binary verdict (did the outcome clear the per-type bar ω_τ, yes or no).

So ō is the graded signal and ô is its thresholded yes/no. Standing updates use the binary ô; credit minting uses the continuous ō. Every formula below uses exactly one of the two, never interchangeably.

3.2 Reviewer standing s_r(t)

Standing is a reviewer's track record of being right. It is bounded in [0,1] so it can be read directly as a weight (1 = consistently confirmed by outcomes, 0 = consistently falsified). It is not transferable and is not a token.

Each time reviewer r's decision on a submission x is later resolved by its outcome (see the next subsection), we get a per-decision correctness signal g_r(x) ∈ [0,1]:

g_r(x) = 1 - | v_r(x) - ô(x) |

where v_r(x) ∈ {0,1} is the binary vote the reviewer cast (1 = accept, 0 = reject) and ô(x) ∈ {0,1} is the resolved binary outcome verdict (1 = the work held / the bug was real and the fix stuck, 0 = it did not). So g_r(x) = 1 when the reviewer's call matched reality and 0 when it was exactly wrong. (If one uses the continuous strength ō(x) ∈ [0,1] in place of the thresholded ô(x), the same formula gives partial correctness.)

Standing updates as an exponential moving average (EMA) over resolved decisions, in resolution order. Let r's k-th resolved decision have correctness g_r,k. Then

s_r(k) = (1 - α) · s_r(k-1) + α · g_r,k

with learning rate α ∈ (0,1). A larger α makes standing react faster to recent decisions; a smaller α makes it stickier. We initialise new reviewers at s_r(0) = s_0, a deliberately low onboarding value (e.g. s_0 = 0.1), so authority is earned, not granted. This is the formal version of the draft's "new reviewers start at low standing."

The EMA has the recency property the draft asks for: the weight on the decision n steps back is α(1-α)^n, which decays geometrically. Old mistakes fade; recent ones dominate. This is also the lever against cold-start entrenchment: early reviewers cannot bank standing forever by being right once.

Pilot mapping: s_r(t) is the confidence the system places in a given admin's bug confirmations. In the early pilot there is one trusted reviewer [PILOT CAPABILITY: operator to confirm reviewer-pool size in deployment], so s_r ≈ 1 by assumption and the weighting is degenerate. The EMA only does real work once |R| > 1 and reviewers disagree. [PILOT RESULT: to be measured, reviewer standing trajectories require ≥2 active reviewers.]

3.3 Decision aggregation and the accept threshold

A submission is not accepted by a head-count of reviewers; it is accepted by a standing-weighted vote of its assigned set A(x). Define the weighted accept share

            Σ_{r ∈ A(x)} s_r(t) · v_r(x)
  W(x)  =  ───────────────────────────────
              Σ_{r ∈ A(x)} s_r(t)

W(x) ∈ [0,1] is the fraction of standing-weight that voted accept. The submission is provisionally accepted iff

  W(x) ≥ θ_τ      and      Σ_{r ∈ A(x)} s_r(t) ≥ q_τ

The first condition is the acceptance threshold θ_τ (e.g. θ_τ = 0.5 for a simple weighted majority; raise it to 0.67 for high-value types). The second is a standing quorum q_τ: enough total trusted weight must be present for the decision to count at all. The quorum stops a single low-standing reviewer from rubber-stamping work, and it forces high-value types to draw in reviewers who actually have standing at risk.

Both θ_τ and q_τ are governance parameters, set per type by the community (Section 4.3). Conflict rule: i(x) ∉ A(x), you cannot review your own submission, and assignment includes a randomisation step so a contributor cannot pick a friendly reviewer.

Pilot mapping: with one reviewer of standing ≈1, W(x) ∈ {0,1} and the quorum is trivially met, so acceptance reduces to "the admin confirmed the bug." The formalism is forward-compatible: adding reviewers changes only A(x), not the rule.

3.4 Outcome-anchored validation o(x) and the window W(τ)

This is the crux the critique flagged, and the part the model treats most carefully. Acceptance above is provisional. It is confirmed only by an outcome that resolves after the work, within a type-specific window.

For each type τ define an outcome window W(τ), a duration (in days) over which the contribution's effect is observed: short for technical fixes (the fix either held or regressed within days), long for content or onboarding (sustained effect measured over weeks). Define the outcome function

  o(x) ∈ [0,1]

measured at the end of the window W(τ(x)). o(x) is the operational signal that the work actually held. Its definition is type-specific and explicitly imperfect:

  • bug_report: o(x) = 1 if the reported defect was reproduced and a fix shipped and did not regress within W(τ); 0 if it was not a real bug, a duplicate, or the "fix" was reverted. This one is close to objective.
  • moderation / onboarding / content: o(x) is a bounded composite of available signals (did the reported problem recur, did the onboarded users stay active, did the content sustain engagement) normalised to [0,1]. This is where the mechanism is weakest and the draft must stay honest: for soft work o(x) is itself a proxy and is itself gameable. We do not claim to have solved outcome measurement for soft contributions; we claim only to move the measurement after the work and outside the contributor's direct control, which raises the cost of gaming relative to activity counts. See the incentive analysis.

From o(x) we read the two glyphs of Section 3.1: the continuous strength ō(x) = o(x), and the resolved binary verdict ô(x) = 1[o(x) ≥ ω_τ] against the per-type outcome bar ω_τ.

Confirm-or-claw-back, with the window anchor stated once. Let t_0 be the time of provisional acceptance (the review/decision time, not the submission time). When a submission is provisionally accepted at t_0, it is recorded but its credit is held in escrow. At t_0 + W(τ) the window closes and resolution occurs:

  • if ô(x) = 1, the credit is confirmed and added to c_i (next subsection), and each reviewer's correctness g_r(x) is computed and folded into their standing;
  • if ô(x) = 0, the credit is clawed back (never added to c_i), the contributor's escrow is voided, and reviewers who voted accept take the standing hit (their g_r(x) is low because v_r = 1 but ô = 0).

This two-phase commit is what ties reviewer incentives to reality: a reviewer who approves work that does not hold loses standing when the window closes. Credit and standing both move only on resolution, never on the vote alone. (The window is anchored at review time t_0, used consistently in Section 5 as well; submission time is recorded separately for latency reporting but is not the credit anchor.)

Pilot mapping: o(x) for the bug loop is "was it really a bug and did the fix hold," judged at the close of W(bug_report) (days, not weeks). The escrow-then-confirm step is exactly the pilot's "durable credit is recorded but payout is anchored to the fix sticking." 3.5 Durable credit c_i

Credit is the contributor's cumulative, non-transferable track record and the basis for reward. It is monotonic non-decreasing (it never falls once confirmed) and non-expiring (the draft's "durable, portable across time"). Non-transferability is what stops identity farming: you cannot sell or move credit, so a Sybil account's credit is worthless to anyone but the account.

When submission x by contributor i is confirmed at the close of its window, it adds

  Δc_i(x)  =  β_τ · ō(x) · W̄(x)

to c_i, where:

  • β_τ > 0 is a per-type base weight (a hard bug fix is worth more than a one-line doc edit; set by governance),
  • ō(x) ∈ [0,1] is the continuous outcome strength of Section 3.1 (so a fix that fully held adds more than one that partly held),
  • W̄(x) ∈ [0,1] is the standing of the deciding panel, the same standing-weighted accept share W(x) from the aggregation step, so a contribution blessed by high-standing reviewers carries more credit than one waved through by low-standing reviewers.

Total credit is the running sum of confirmed contributions, where "confirmed" is the binary verdict ô(x) = 1:

  c_i  =  Σ_{x : i(x)=i, ô(x)=1}  β_{τ(x)} · ō(x) · W̄(x)

Note the two glyphs working together: the set of terms is restricted by the binary ô(x)=1 (only confirmed submissions count), and each term's magnitude uses the continuous ō(x) (graded strength). Because every term is non-negative and terms are only ever added on confirmation (never on the provisional vote), c_i is monotonic non-decreasing by construction. Clawed-back submissions (ô=0) contribute zero: they were never summed in.

Pilot mapping: c_i is the contributor's recorded bug-and-earn credit plus the receipt they keep. Each confirmed bug adds Δc_i weighted by how hard the bug was (β_τ), how cleanly the fix held (ō), and the standing of the confirming reviewer (W̄). 3.6 Reward distribution from the treasury T_e

At epoch e the treasury holds a balance T_e (funded by the flat per-activity fee, Section 4.1). A governance-set fraction of that balance is earmarked for contributors; call the contributor pool P_e, with P_e = γ · T_e for a governance-set split fraction γ ∈ (0,1]. The pool P_e (not the full balance T_e) is split pro-rata by credit share:

                    c_i
  reward_i(e)  =  ──────── · P_e
                   Σ_j c_j

A contributor holding 5% of all outstanding credit receives 5% of the epoch's contributor pool P_e. This is the SourceCred / AMM-LP-fee shape, redirected from capital to labour: value tracks confirmed contribution, not tokens held.

Recency-weighted variant (anti-entrenchment). Pure cumulative credit rewards early contributors forever, even after they stop contributing, the cold-start / whale-of-credit problem the draft names. To fight this, define a recency-discounted credit used only for distribution (the durable c_i is untouched):

  c_i^{(e)}  =  Σ_{x : i(x)=i, ô(x)=1}  Δc_i(x) · λ^{(e - e(x))}

where e(x) is the epoch in which x confirmed and λ ∈ (0,1] is a decay factor. With λ = 1 this is the plain cumulative split above. With λ < 1, credit earned long ago is discounted for payout purposes only, so the reward share

                    c_i^{(e)}
  reward_i(e)  =  ───────────── · P_e
                   Σ_j c_j^{(e)}

shifts toward contributors who are still active, while the permanent record c_i (track record, status) stays monotonic and intact. λ is the single knob trading off "honour the founders" against "don't let them collect rent forever," and it is a governance parameter. This separates credit-as-history (durable, never decays) from credit-as-claim-on-this-epoch (recency-weighted), which the prose draft conflated. The second quantity is allowed to fall because it is a payout weight, not the permanent credit ledger.

3.7 Incentive analysis

Two honesty questions matter: do contributors report truthfully, and do reviewers judge truthfully? The mechanism makes honesty the better strategy only inside a stated regime. We state it plainly, including where it fails.

Honest reporting (contributor side). A contributor chooses whether to submit genuine work or fabricated/low-effort work hoping it slips through. Let:

  • p = probability a fabricated submission is caught (rejected at review, or accepted-then-clawed-back when ô(x)=0),
  • b = the credit gained if a fabrication is wrongly confirmed,
  • κ = the cost of fabricating evidence convincing enough to clear review,
  • and note a fabrication that is caught yields zero credit (no c_i increment) and burns the fabrication cost κ.

Expected payoff from fabricating is (1-p)·b - κ. Honest reporting weakly dominates fabrication when

  (1 - p) · b  ≤  κ          (honest-reporting condition)

i.e. when catching is likely enough and/or fabricating convincing evidence is expensive enough that the expected illicit gain does not cover its cost. The two levers are p (review quality + outcome anchoring + audit rate) and κ (evidence bar). Outcome anchoring raises p specifically: even evidence that fools a reviewer at vote time still has to survive the window. A "bug" that was never real fails o(x) and is clawed back, so the contributor cannot bank b by gaming the review alone, only by gaming the outcome, which is harder and later.

Honest review (reviewer side). A reviewer chooses to judge truthfully or to collude (approve a contributor's bad work for a bribe β). Colluding means voting v_r = 1 on an x that will fail its outcome. When the window closes with ô(x)=0, the reviewer's correctness g_r(x) is low, dragging down s_r via the EMA. Lower standing means lower vote weight, fewer/less-valuable assignments, and, if standing carries a stake or a share of reviewer rewards, direct future loss. Let:

  • β = the bribe,
  • L_r = the present value of the reviewer's future earnings lost to the standing hit from one caught bad approval,
  • q = the probability the bad approval is in fact resolved against the reviewer (it reaches the window / an audit samples it).

Honest review dominates collusion when

  q · L_r  ≥  β            (honest-review condition)

i.e. when the expected standing-at-risk exceeds the bribe. This is why standing must be slow to earn and meaningful to lose, and why audits matter: audits raise q for decisions whose natural outcome signal is weak (exactly the soft-work case).

Where it is NOT incentive-compatible, stated honestly.

  1. Not strategy-proof. Neither condition holds universally. If a fabrication is cheap (κ small) and rarely caught (p small), or a bribe β exceeds a low-standing reviewer's at-risk value L_r (e.g. a fresh reviewer with s_0 and nothing banked), the honest strategy loses. The mechanism is incentive-aligned in a regime, not incentive-proof.
  1. Soft outcomes are the soft underbelly. The whole p-raising argument leans on o(x) actually detecting bad work. For bug_report it largely does. For moderation/onboarding/content, o(x) is a proxy and is itself gameable (Goodhart's law: once o(x) is the target, contributors optimise the proxy, not the underlying value). The model does not claim to defeat this; it claims only to move measurement after the act and outside the contributor's hand, and to backstop weak o(x) with audits (raising q) and with rubric rotation (so the proxy is a moving target).
  1. Low-standing reviewers are cheap to bribe. The honest-review condition is weakest exactly at onboarding, when L_r is small. Mitigation is structural, not formal: route high-value submissions only to panels meeting the standing quorum q_τ, and keep new reviewers on low-stakes, easily-outcome-verified types until they have standing to lose.
  1. Collusion at scale. The conditions assume independent reviewers and honest audits. A cartel that captures both the review panel and the audit sample breaks both conditions; the formal model cannot fix governance capture (the threat model in Section 6 and the Governance section carry that load: separation of contribution governance from token governance, randomised assignment, influence caps).

The regime where honesty holds, stated as one line: honesty is the better strategy when fabricating convincing evidence is costly, outcomes are anchored on a signal the contributor cannot fully control, reviewers have meaningful standing at risk, and the audit probability is high enough to keep q·L_r ≥ β even for soft work. Outside that regime, cheap fakes, weak proxies, fresh reviewers, captured audits, the mechanism degrades to "trust the humans," which is precisely the limitation the paper already admits: this is not trustless.

4. The System

The mechanism has two functional layers and one governance shell. The economic base (4.1) raises and accounts for the treasury. The contribution layer (4.2) converts judged work into durable credit and distributes the treasury against it. The governance shell (4.3) sets the parameters of both and adjudicates disputes. The formal objects of Section 3 sit inside this structure; this section is the plain-language wiring.

4.1 Economic base

The economic base is deliberately simple, because simplicity is what makes the accounting auditable.

Flat fee per unit of activity. A constant fee accrues on each unit of activity in the system. The fee is flat, a fixed amount per unit, not a percentage of value moved. A flat fee is predictable, is not gamed by inflating transaction size, and is easy to verify against on-chain records.

Fees accrue to a treasury, not to liquidity providers. This is the distributional reversal that motivates the paper. In a conventional automated-market-maker design, the per-trade fee accrues to liquidity providers, rewarding capital for being present. Here the same fee stream is redirected into a community treasury that funds contributor rewards. Capital is no longer the default recipient of activity fees; labour is.

No inflationary emissions. Contributor rewards are paid out of fees actually collected, not out of newly minted supply. There is no emission schedule diluting holders to pay contributors. The treasury can only distribute what it has taken in, which bounds the reward pool to real economic activity and removes the death-spiral failure mode of emission-funded reward programs.

Transparent accounting. Treasury inflows (fees collected) and outflows (epoch distributions) are recorded transparently so any participant can reconstruct the balance and the split. Transparency is itself a control: it lets the community detect a treasury that is being drained or a split that does not match governance.

The treasury T_e of Section 3.6 is exactly this balance at epoch e, and the contributor pool P_e = γ · T_e is the earmarked fraction paid out. [FIELD DATA: operator to supply, treasury inflow rate and epoch cadence once payout goes live on test funds; no live payout numbers exist yet.]

4.2 Contribution layer

The contribution layer is the path from a unit of work to a unit of durable credit and, eventually, to a share of the treasury. It has five stages, each formalised in Section 3.

  1. Structured claim. A contributor submits a structured claim of completed work, with evidence, under a defined contribution type τ. The claim is a durable, evidence-bearing record (the submission x), not an ephemeral message.
  1. Standing-weighted review. Reviewers with standing, drawn by randomised assignment from the eligible pool and excluding the contributor, evaluate the claim against the type's outcome rubric. Acceptance is a standing-weighted vote that must clear both the acceptance threshold θ_τ and the standing quorum q_τ (Section 3.3).
  1. Outcome anchoring with a window. Acceptance is provisional. The claim is confirmed only if its outcome o(x) holds at the close of the type's outcome window W(τ), measured from review time t_0 (Section 3.4). Credit is held in escrow until then and is clawed back if the outcome fails, which is what ties reviewer standing to reality.
  1. Durable credit. On confirmation, the contributor's identity-bound credit c_i increases by Δc_i(x), weighted by type base weight, outcome strength ō(x), and the standing of the confirming panel (Section 3.5). Credit is non-transferable, non-expiring, and monotonic. The contributor also keeps a receipt linking the credit to its justification.
  1. Pro-rata distribution. Each epoch, the contributor pool P_e of the treasury is split pro-rata to credit share, with an optional recency discount λ that shifts payout toward still-active contributors without erasing the permanent record (Section 3.6).

The load-bearing design choice across all five stages is that nothing pays for activity. A claim that is filed but not confirmed mints nothing; a confirmation that does not survive its window mints nothing. Value attaches only to outcomes that held.

4.3 Governance

Governance is the shell that sets every parameter the layers above leave open, and it is built on Ostrom's design principles (Section 2.2). The community, not a central planner, decides:

  • Categories and rubrics. Which contribution types τ exist, and the outcome rubric and outcome bar ω_τ for each.
  • Outcome windows. The window length W(τ) per type, short for technical fixes, long for content and onboarding.
  • Distribution cadence and split. The epoch length, the fraction γ of the treasury earmarked for contributors (forming the pool P_e), and the recency decay λ.
  • Acceptance parameters. The acceptance threshold θ_τ and standing quorum q_τ per type.

Three governance rules protect the contribution layer from capture, and they are the same rules the threat model (Section 6) leans on:

  1. Reviewer onboarding starts at low standing. New reviewers enter at s_0 on a dedicated lane, shadow-judging alongside established reviewers with initially non-binding weight, and graduate by demonstrated accuracy rather than by social approval from incumbents. This keeps the reviewer pool refreshing and bounds first-mover advantage.
  1. Appeals adjudicated by higher-standing reviewers. A contributor or reviewer may appeal a decision; the appeal is adjudicated by reviewers of higher standing than the original panel. Overturned decisions feed back into the standing of the reviewers who got it wrong.
  1. Periodic audits and recalibration. A random sample of accepted decisions is re-judged blind to catch corruption that the natural outcome signal missed (raising q in the incentive analysis), and rubrics, windows, and standing baselines are re-examined on a fixed cadence to correct drift introduced by the founding cohort.

Critically, contribution governance is separated from token-weighted governance. Token weight may set treasury-level economic parameters, but it does not select reviewers, set rubrics, or adjudicate appeals. A large token holder's capital buys no votes over the layer that pays humans. This firewall is what stops the economic base from re-concentrating value in capital through the back door of governance (Section 6.5).

5. Measuring Outcomes Without Creating New Targets

The mechanism in Section 4 rests on one load-bearing claim: validation is outcome-anchored, not activity-anchored. We reward whether a contribution did what it was supposed to do, not whether it happened. This is the part of the design most likely to be wrong, because for several contribution classes the outcome is itself hard to observe and, once observed, becomes a metric that can be gamed. This section specifies the outcome, window, signal source, and gaming risk for each class, then confronts the Goodhart problem (Goodhart 1975) directly and honestly: outcome anchoring narrows the attack surface, it does not close it.

We reuse the Section 3 notation throughout: a contribution is a submission x of type τ(x); its binary resolved outcome is ô(x) = 1[o(x) ≥ ω_τ] ∈ {0,1} (resolved / not resolved), measured only after the type window W(τ) has elapsed. Credit is minted at confirmation time t_0 + W(τ), where t_0 is the review/acceptance time of Section 3.4, not the submission time. The delay is deliberate and is itself a defense (Section 5.8).

5.1 Design rule: an outcome is something that could have failed

Before specifying each class, we state the rule that separates an outcome from an activity. An outcome is a proposition that could have come out false after the work was accepted. "The user posted 12 messages" cannot come out false; it is a count. "The reported defect did not recur within W(τ) days" can come out false, and frequently does. Every row in the specifications below is chosen so that the signal can contradict the contributor's claim. Where no falsifiable signal exists for a class, we say so and fall back to standing-weighted human judgement (Section 5.6) rather than inventing a proxy count.

5.2 Contribution classes

The five classes below cover the pilot's current and planned surface. The bug-fix class is the only one with a running intake/review implementation, with capability markers for what still requires operator confirmation; the others are specified here and instrumented as the pilot expands. Where a row describes the currently piloted intake/review path we mark it as pilot; where it describes intended instrumentation we mark it as planned. All capability-sensitive claims remain marked until confirmed by row proof. Unless stated otherwise, every class window is anchored at provisional acceptance time t_0, not submission time.

Class 1, Bug fix / technical report (pilot, with capability placeholders)

  • Outcome: the reported defect is real, is resolved, and does not regress within the window; the reporter confirms the fix on their own path.
  • Window W(τ): days. Short, because software defects either recur quickly under real traffic or do not.
  • Signal source: the issue tracker (state transition to resolved), an explicit re-test against the originally failing path, and application or on-chain telemetry showing the failing event no longer fires. In the pilot design this resolves to a durable credit row keyed on the report identity and the case identity, written only after an admin/maintainer confirmation; whether idempotent credit, duplicate collapse, provenance checks, and standing tracking are active is explicitly left as a pilot-capability check below.
  • Gaming risk: a contributor reports a defect they themselves introduced or know is about to be fixed; or splits one defect into many reports to multiply credit.
  • Mitigation (intended): credit is intended to be idempotent on (reporter, case) so one defect mints at most one credit per reporter; reviewer standing is staked on the "genuinely new" judgement; provenance of the underlying change is checked so self-introduced defects do not pay. Duplicate and near-duplicate reports collapse to the first. [PILOT CAPABILITY: operator to confirm in deployment which of these mitigations, idempotent (reporter, case) credit, provenance checking, duplicate collapse, are live versus planned.]

Class 2, Moderation (planned)

  • Outcome: the reported problem is resolved and repeat incidents of the same type decline; the moderation action did not simply displace the problem.
  • Window W(τ): weeks. Long enough that suppression and recurrence separate from a one-time removal.
  • Signal source: incident rate for the relevant category before versus after the action, measured over matched windows; reopen rate on the same actors or threads.
  • Gaming risk: a moderator manufactures incidents to then resolve them; or over-moderates to drive the visible incident count down while harming the community (the metric improves, the thing it stood for does not).
  • Mitigation: incident rate is one signal in an ensemble (Section 5.7), paired with a community-health signal the moderator does not control; the before-window is unannounced (Section 5.4); standing-weighted human review is the backstop where the count is silent on quality.

Class 3, Onboarding (planned)

  • Outcome: the onboarded participant remains active past the window and themselves makes a confirmed contribution.
  • Window W(τ): weeks to months. The longest window, because onboarding only pays if it produced a durable, productive participant, not a one-day arrival.
  • Signal source: retention of the onboarded identity past the window and at least one downstream contribution by that identity that itself clears its own class's outcome check.
  • Gaming risk: Sybil onboarding, where the onboarder controls the onboarded identities (Douceur 2002); or coaching a participant through a single token contribution to trigger payout.
  • Mitigation: the downstream contribution must itself be outcome-confirmed, so a coached token action that fails its own window does not pay the onboarder; the onboarded identity is subject to the same identity and standing checks as any contributor, which raises the cost of Sybil onboarding to the cost of Sybil contribution.

Class 4, Content / education (planned)

  • Outcome: sustained engagement or a measurable downstream action, not vanity views; a reader did something the content was meant to enable.
  • Window W(τ): weeks.
  • Signal source: durable engagement (return visits over the window, not first-view spikes) and completion of the task the content teaches, where such a task exists (e.g. a guide whose readers subsequently complete the guided action).
  • Gaming risk: view inflation, bot engagement, and the classic vanity-metric trap where reach is bought.
  • Mitigation: first-view spikes are explicitly discounted in favor of return engagement and task completion; engagement counts from identities that have themselves passed identity checks; where comprehension cannot be measured, this class falls back hardest to standing-weighted human judgement and is the weakest of the five (we say so plainly).

Class 5, Localization / docs (planned)

  • Outcome: the translation or documentation is adopted and reduces the support load it was meant to reduce.
  • Window W(τ): weeks.
  • Signal source: adoption (the artifact is actually served to and used by speakers of the target language) and the change in support-ticket volume for the topic the artifact covers, before versus after.
  • Gaming risk: machine-translating volume to mint many low-value artifacts; or producing docs for problems no one has.
  • Mitigation: the support-ticket delta only moves if real users were actually blocked and then were not, so volume without demand pays nothing; adoption must be observed, not asserted; a reviewer who speaks the target language confirms fitness.

5.3 The Goodhart problem, stated plainly

Every signal source above is a metric, and every outcome metric, once it pays, becomes a target (Goodhart 1975; Strathern's restatement: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure). Moving from activity to outcomes does not escape this law. It only moves the target one layer deeper, from "did you act" to "did your act produce the measured outcome." A sufficiently motivated adversary will optimize the outcome metric instead of the underlying good, exactly as they previously optimized the activity count. Outcome-flavored reward programs in the retroactive public-goods space (e.g. Optimism's Retro Funding program and Gitcoin's grant rounds (Optimism Documentation, n.d.; Gitcoin, 2026)) are widely discussed as surfacing this in practice: even retrospective reward attracts optimization toward whatever the reviewers visibly weigh.

We therefore do not claim outcome anchoring is gaming-proof. We hypothesize that it is gaming-harder than activity counting, and Section 8 defines the measurements that must prove or falsify that hypothesis. The design layers five defenses that each aim to raise the cost of optimizing the metric away from the good. None is sufficient alone, and several are specified here but not yet deployed (Section 9); we tag each defense accordingly.

5.4 Defense 1, Hold-out and unannounced metrics (planned, not yet deployed)

For each class, part of the outcome signal is not disclosed at submission time. The intended design is that the contributor knows the kind of outcome that will be judged (the rubric is public, per Section 4.3 governance) but not the exact window boundary, the exact comparison cohort, or which of several ensemble signals will carry the most weight in a given period. A held-out signal cannot be optimized against directly because the adversary does not know its current shape. This is the standard hold-out idea from evaluation practice applied to incentive design: you cannot teach to a test you cannot see. The cost is real and we state it: hold-outs reduce contributor legibility ("why was I judged this way?") and must be reconciled against the appeals process (Section 4.3) so that opacity does not become arbitrariness. This defense is part of the design for the soft classes, all of which are currently planned rather than active in the pilot (Section 5.2).

5.5 Defense 2, Rotating rubrics (planned, not yet deployed)

The exact rubric weights within a class rotate on a cadence the community sets, within published bounds. An adversary who reverse-engineers this period's weighting finds it stale next period. Rotation trades a small amount of consistency for a moving target. It is bounded, not random: the categories are stable and public; only the emphasis moves, so honest contributors are not whipsawed while gamers cannot lock onto a fixed objective. As with Defense 1, rubric rotation applies to the multi-signal soft classes and is not yet exercised by the pilot bug-report class.

5.6 Defense 3, Standing-weighted human judgement as the irreducible backstop

For the classes where the metric is weakest (content comprehension, moderation quality), a human reviewer with standing renders a qualitative judgement that the metric cannot fully replace. The intended formal backstop is the peer-prediction insight (Miller, Resnick and Zeckhauser 2005; Prelec 2004): when ground truth is delayed or partly unobservable, a scoring rule over informed human judgements, with the judges themselves staked on long-run accuracy, recovers signal that no automatic count can. This future peer-prediction scoring layer is specified as the soft-work backstop but is not yet deployed (Section 9); today the backstop is the staked-reviewer judgement itself, without the peer-prediction scoring rule attached. Standing is the stake. A reviewer who rubber-stamps gamed work loses standing when the outcome later fails to hold; a reviewer whose hard calls are later vindicated gains it. The backstop is not "trust the human"; it is "trust the human whose past judgements have been outcome-checked, and keep checking." This is the same staked-reviewer machinery as the rest of the mechanism, pointed at the residue the metrics miss.

5.7 Defense 4, Outcome ensembles (planned, not yet deployed)

No class is judged on a single signal. Each combines several weak signals that an adversary would have to corrupt jointly. For moderation: incident rate and reopen rate and an independent community-health signal. For content: return engagement and downstream task completion. For localization: adoption and support-ticket delta. Corrupting one weak signal is cheap; corrupting all of them coherently, in the same direction, without tripping the human backstop, is expensive. Ensembles are the EigenTrust intuition (Kamvar, Schlosser and Garcia-Molina 2003) at the signal layer rather than the identity layer: aggregate many partial views so that no single corruptible source dominates. The ensembles described here belong to the planned soft classes (Section 5.2) and are not yet instrumented in the pilot loop.

5.8 Defense 5, Delayed and retroactive confirmation (pilot target for the bug-fix class)

Credit mints at t_0 + W(τ), never at review time t_0 itself and never at submission. A short-term spike that decays inside the window pays nothing. This directly defeats the farm-and-dump pattern the paper opens against: a burst of activity that looks like contribution but does not hold up does not survive its own window. Retroactive confirmation also lets the system pay for outcomes that were not anticipated at submission (the RetroPGF and Hypercerts logic: reward what demonstrably held, after it has held (Optimism Documentation, n.d.; Hypercerts Foundation, n.d.)), and it gives the human backstop time to see the longer-run consequence the metric alone would miss. The cost is latency: contributors wait for credit, which we accept as the price of not paying for spikes. Of the five defenses, this is the only one scoped to the pilot bug-fix class; its measured performance is still a pilot result placeholder, and the others (5.4, 5.5, 5.7, and the peer-prediction layer of 5.6) are specified but not yet deployed.

5.9 Honest limits

These five defenses, once deployed, reduce gaming; they do not eliminate it. Three residual exposures remain, and we name them rather than paper over them:

  1. The metric is still a target. A patient adversary who can wait out the window, satisfy the ensemble, and survive human review has produced something close to the real good, which is the point, but a well-resourced one can still extract more than they contribute at the margin. Outcome anchoring raises the cost of extraction; it does not invert it.
  2. Soft classes lean on human judgement, which is finite and itself attackable. Reviewer collusion and bribery (addressed in the threat model) bear directly on Section 5.6 and the qualitative parts of every class. The standing mechanism is the defense, and it is only as strong as the long-run outcome checks that update standing. The peer-prediction scoring rule that would formalize this backstop is not yet deployed.
  3. Some outcomes are genuinely unobservable on any practical window. Where that is true we prefer to under-reward (and say a class is weak) rather than mint credit against a proxy count, because a proxy count is exactly the activity metric this paper exists to replace.

6. Threat Model

The mechanism in Sections 4 and 5 mints durable, identity-bound credit and pays a treasury against it. Any system that converts judged work into recurring value is a target. We do not claim the mechanism is trustless or that gaming is impossible; it depends on human review and off-chain signals, and a sufficiently funded, patient adversary can defeat any of the controls below in isolation.

The design goal is narrower and is the organizing principle for this entire section:

Make the expected cost of gaming exceed the expected reward from honest contribution, for every attack class, simultaneously.

Honest contribution has a floor cost (do the work) and a ceiling reward (your pro-rata share of the treasury). An attack is rational only when its expected payoff clears its expected cost, where cost includes identity acquisition, reviewer corruption, the discount for getting caught, and the standing forfeited on detection. Each subsection below names an attack, then states the mechanism-level control that raises one of those cost terms. Several controls are designs rather than pilot deployment functions; we tag those explicitly and we mark [PILOT RESULT: ...] or [PILOT CAPABILITY: ...] wherever a real number or a deployment confirmation is required to claim a control works (or even exists) rather than is merely intended.

We treat six attack classes: Sybil identity and credit farming; reviewer collusion and bribery; reviewer cartel and entrenchment; outcome-signal poisoning; whale capture; and cold-start bias.

6.1 Sybil identity and credit farming

Attack. One human controls N identities, each submitting work (or near-duplicate work), to capture N shares of treasury credit for one person's effort. The pure form is the Sybil attack (Douceur, 2002): in any system without a trusted identity authority, a single party can present arbitrarily many identities, so any reward proportional to identity count is farmable for the cost of creating identities.

Why credit is the prize. Credit here is non-transferable and non-expiring (Section 3.5). That removes resale markets but raises the incentive to farm identities directly, because each fake identity is a permanent claim on every future treasury distribution, not a one-time payout. The control surface must therefore attack identity supply, not just identity behavior.

Controls (cost terms they raise):

  1. Cost-imposing identity (raises acquisition cost). Account creation is gated by a proof-of-personhood signal: a per-account cost that is cheap once and expensive N times. We do not require a single global personhood oracle. We accept a menu, an external proof-of-personhood attestation, a stake bond that is slashable on detected farming, or a vouching edge from an already-established identity, and weight an account's maximum admissible credit rate by the strength of the personhood evidence it carries. A bare account can submit, but its per-period credit is capped low until it accrues stronger evidence. This is the standard move: convert "one human, many identities" from free into a per-identity bond.
  1. **Web-of-trust attestations (raises the cost of isolated identities).** New identities enter a vouching graph. Vouching is itself a contribution edge: the voucher's standing is partially at risk if the vouchee is later found to be a farm. This is EigenTrust's transitive-trust structure (Kamvar, Schlosser, Garcia-Molina, 2003) applied to personhood rather than file sharing: trust flows along attestation edges from a pre-trusted seed set, and a cluster of mutually-vouching fresh accounts with no edge to the seed set carries near-zero transitive trust regardless of internal density. A Sybil farm can fake its internal edges for free; it cannot fake an edge from an established identity that has standing to lose.
  1. Progressive evidence-quality requirements per account (raises marginal cost as count grows). The evidence bar for an accepted contribution rises with the volume an account submits relative to its standing. A first contribution from a low-standing account may be accepted on a screenshot and a reviewer confirmation; the tenth in a window from the same low-standing account requires reproducible evidence (a failing test, a linked artifact, a third-party-checkable outcome). The honest high-volume contributor pays this once by earning standing, which relaxes the bar. The farm pays it per identity, forever, because each fresh identity starts at the bottom of the curve. This is the term that makes farming super-linear in cost while honest work stays linear.
  1. **Duplicate-submission detection (collapses N near-identical claims to one).** Submissions are fingerprinted on content and on outcome target. Text claims are near-duplicate-matched; image evidence is perceptual-hash matched against a duplicate index so that re-uploaded or lightly-edited evidence collides with the original. Two accounts reporting the same bug, or one account re-reporting it under a synonym, are routed to the same claim and can mint credit once. [PILOT CAPABILITY: operator to confirm in deployment whether perceptual image-hash duplicate indexing on submitted evidence is live; if live, re-used evidence across accounts is a detectable collision rather than two payouts.] [PILOT RESULT: duplicate-collision rate among submissions, to be measured].

Residual risk. A patient adversary who acquires N genuinely distinct personhood proofs and does N distinct real units of work has defeated this class, but at that point they are an honest high-volume contributor by construction, and the treasury is paying for N real outcomes. That is the intended failure mode: the cheapest way to extract credit at scale becomes indistinguishable from contributing. This is also where Douceur's impossibility (Section 2.4) bites: non-transferable credit raises the cost of farming onto the work, but it does not make fabricating N genuine identities-plus-work impossible.

6.2 Reviewer collusion and bribery

Attack. A contributor bribes, or is secretly identical to, the reviewer who judges their claim. The reviewer stamps fabricated or low-quality work as a held outcome; both split the treasury payout. Because review is the trust hinge of the whole mechanism, a corrupted reviewer is worth more to an attacker than a corrupted contributor.

Controls:

  1. Multi-reviewer random assignment (raises the number of parties that must be corrupted). A claim is assigned to reviewers drawn randomly from the eligible reviewer pool, not chosen by the contributor and not self-selected by reviewers. To pre-arrange a corrupt review, the attacker must corrupt reviewers before knowing which ones will be drawn, i.e. corrupt a large fraction of the pool. Random assignment converts "bribe one known reviewer" into "bribe enough of the pool that you are likely to draw a corrupt quorum."
  1. Standing-weighted quorum (raises the standing that must be at risk). Acceptance requires a quorum whose summed standing clears a threshold, not a simple head-count. A claim cannot be carried by a clutch of low-standing accounts. To force an accept, the attacker must control reviewers who collectively hold significant standing, and standing is exactly what those reviewers forfeit when the decision is later falsified.
  1. Standing decay on falsified decisions (prices the bribe). When a later outcome check or appeal overturns a decision, every reviewer who voted for the overturned outcome loses standing, scaled to how confidently they backed it. This makes standing a bond posted on each vote. A reviewer's bribe price is now bounded below by the discounted value of the standing they expect to lose times the probability of being caught. Raising the catch probability (controls 4 and 5) directly raises the floor bribe price.
  1. Blinded audits sampling decisions (raises catch probability without auditing everything). A random sample of accepted decisions is re-judged blind: the auditor does not see the original reviewers' identities or votes, and ideally re-checks the outcome directly. Sampling means the attacker cannot predict which decisions get a second look, so every corrupt decision carries a standing audit probability p > 0. Combined with control 3, the expected cost of a corrupt vote is at least p × (standing forfeited); the community sets p against its audit budget. [PILOT RESULT: blinded-audit sample rate and overturn rate, to be measured].
  1. Whistle-blower rewards (turns the cartel against itself). A party with inside knowledge of a corrupt decision, including a member of the colluding group, can report it for a reward funded from the slashed standing and forfeited credit of the guilty parties. This makes every collusion agreement unstable: each member's dominant strategy, once the work is paid, is to defect and claim the bounty before a co-conspirator does. The control does not need to catch collusion externally; it needs to make staying silent the worse bet for at least one insider.
  1. **Future control: peer-prediction scoring (truthful review without ground truth), designed, not yet deployed.** For the large class of decisions where no clean later outcome exists, we cannot score reviewers against reality directly. The intended design is to score them against each other using a peer-prediction / Bayesian-truth-serum rule (Miller, Resnick, Zeckhauser, 2005; Prelec, 2004): each reviewer reports a judgement and a prediction of how others will judge, and is paid for being surprisingly common, judgements that are more frequent than the population predicted. Under the standard assumptions these rules make truthful reporting a strict best response, so a reviewer maximizes score by reporting what they actually believe rather than coordinating on a pre-agreed lie. If deployed and if its assumptions hold, this would narrow the soft-work gap (see Section 3.7 and Section 9): it would make truthful review incentive-compatible even where outcomes are not directly observable. We state plainly two things. First, these guarantees are equilibrium results under modeling assumptions (common prior, no side-channel coordination) that a determined cartel can violate; peer prediction raises the coordination cost of collusion, it does not eliminate it. Second, this scoring rule is not yet deployed (Section 9); it is a specified backstop, not an operating control today.

Residual risk. A cartel that controls a supermajority of pool standing, coordinates off-platform to beat the peer-prediction rule (once deployed), and self-polices against whistle-blowers can still pass corrupt decisions. The controls in Section 6.3 attack exactly this concentration.

6.3 Reviewer cartel and entrenchment

Attack. Over time a small set of high-standing reviewers comes to judge most claims. Even without explicit collusion, entrenchment is dangerous: a fixed in-group decides who gets paid, new reviewers cannot break in, and the Section 6.2 controls weaken because "the pool" is the cartel. This is the governance analogue of capital capture: standing, once concentrated, defends itself.

Controls:

  1. Recency weighting on standing (standing must be re-earned, not banked). Standing decays with time and is refreshed by recent, correctly-judged decisions. A reviewer who stops being accurate, or stops participating, sees their weight fall. Entrenchment by historical reputation alone is impossible: yesterday's standing does not vote today.
  1. Mandatory reviewer rotation (breaks fixed assignment). Reviewers are rotated across categories and across claims so the same pair does not repeatedly co-judge. Rotation directly attacks the stable relationships a cartel needs and increases the number of distinct reviewers an attacker must reach.
  1. Caps on any single reviewer's share of decisions (bounds concentration). No single reviewer may judge more than a fixed fraction of decisions in a category per window. Above the cap, claims route to others. This is a hard ceiling on how much of the trust surface one entity can hold, independent of how much standing they have accumulated.
  1. New-reviewer onboarding lanes (keeps the pool refreshing). New reviewers enter at low standing on a dedicated lane: they shadow-judge alongside established reviewers, their judgements are scored but initially non-binding, and they graduate to binding weight by accuracy, not by social approval from incumbents. This guarantees a continuous inflow the cartel cannot gate, which is what keeps controls 1 to 3 from operating on a frozen population. [PILOT RESULT: reviewer-pool turnover and new-reviewer graduation rate, to be measured].

Residual risk. Caps and rotation trade some efficiency for decentralization: a small honest community may struggle to fill the pool, which overlaps the cold-start problem in Section 6.6. The community sets the caps; setting them too tight starves throughput, too loose permits entrenchment. This is a tuning surface, not a solved value.

6.4 Outcome-signal poisoning

Attack. The mechanism is outcome-anchored: credit and reviewer standing are settled against an off-chain signal that defines whether the fix held or the problem resolved. If the attacker controls or corrupts that signal, they corrupt every downstream judgement without ever bribing a reviewer: they poison the ground truth itself. This is the most dangerous class because it defeats the Section 6.2 controls by making the falsified decision look correct to honest auditors.

Controls:

  1. Signal ensembles (no single point of truth), planned, not yet deployed. The intended design reads the outcome for a contribution type from multiple independent signals, not one. A "the bug is fixed" outcome might combine a passing regression test, the absence of re-reports over the outcome window, and a maintainer confirmation. An attacker must corrupt a quorum of independent signals, not one feed. Ensembling raises the cost of poisoning from "compromise one source" to "compromise enough sources to move the aggregate." Multi-signal ensembling is specified for the soft classes and is not yet instrumented (Section 9).
  1. Signing and attestation of outcome data (raises tamper cost to forgery), planned, not yet deployed. The intended design has outcome data carry a signature from its source. A reviewer or auditor checks the signature before trusting the reading, so substituting a fabricated outcome requires forging a source's key, not merely posting a number. Unsigned or unverifiable outcome data is treated as absent, which fails the contribution closed rather than open. Signed outcome attestation is a design target, not yet a deployed control.
  1. Commit hashes on-chain (makes the record append-only and back-dating-proof), planned, not yet deployed for treasury-settled outcomes. The intended design commits the hash of the outcome evidence to an append-only ledger at decision time. Later, anyone can check that the evidence used to settle a claim is byte-identical to what was committed when the decision was made. This blocks the retroactive attack, quietly editing the "outcome" after payout to match a falsified decision, because the original commitment is immutable and timestamped. The pilot's append-only contribution ledger (durable ticket rows with timestamps, Section 7) is the local instance of this commitment surface; the on-chain hash is the stronger form for treasury-settled outcomes and is not yet deployed. [FIELD DATA: operator to supply which outcome signals are ensembled per contribution type in the pilot deployment, if any].

Residual risk. Ensembles, signing, and commitments harden the path from signal to decision; they cannot make a unobservable outcome observable. For soft work whose "outcome" no instrument measures cleanly, the system is designed to fall back on peer prediction (Section 6.2 control 6, not yet deployed) and a conservative outcome window, and we say so honestly in Section 9: the anti-gaming guarantee is strongest exactly where outcomes are measurable and weakest exactly where they are not.

6.5 Whale capture of governance and rewards

Attack. A large token holder buys control of the parameters, categories, rubrics, windows, treasury split, reviewer eligibility, and steers rewards to themselves or their allies. If contribution governance is token-weighted, the same capital that the economic base concentrates can be turned to capture the contribution layer, defeating the paper's premise (that value should reach humans who do the work, not capital). Goodhart's law lurks here too: a captured rubric becomes a target the captor optimizes against.

Controls:

  1. Separate contribution governance from token-weighted governance (two distinct franchises). Decisions about who reviews and what counts as an outcome are not made by token weight. Token-weighted governance may set treasury-level economic parameters; it does not select reviewers, set rubrics, or adjudicate appeals. A whale's tokens buy no votes over the contribution layer. This is a deliberate firewall: the layer that pays humans is governed by contribution standing, not by capital.
  1. Identity-weighted reviewer selection (one human, bounded influence). Reviewer eligibility and quorum weight derive from personhood-anchored standing (Section 6.1, Section 6.2), not from holdings. Buying tokens does not buy standing; standing is earned by accurate judgement and is non-transferable. A whale who wants influence over contribution decisions must actually review accurately, at which point they are subject to every Section 6.2 / 6.3 control.
  1. Cap single-entity influence (hard ceiling across both franchises). No single identity, measured through the personhood and web-of-trust layer, so a whale cannot evade the cap by splitting into wallets, may hold more than a fixed share of reviewer weight or of any single governance decision. This is the same concentration ceiling as Section 6.3 control 3, applied at the governance layer. We separate this from quadratic-style influence-dampening (Buterin, Hitzig, Weyl, 2019) on purpose: quadratic funding presumes robust personhood to resist Sybil splitting, which is precisely the assumption under attack in Section 6.1, so we rely on the cap-plus-personhood combination rather than on a quadratic weighting that a Sybil farm could defeat.

Residual risk. The firewall depends on personhood holding (Section 6.1). If identity is defeated, identity-weighting collapses back toward whoever can mint the most identities, which is why Section 6.1 is load-bearing for this section, not independent of it.

6.6 Cold-start bias

Attack. This is less an adversary than a structural failure that adversaries exploit. Early in a community's life, a few reviewers and a few contributors hold all the standing. Their early judgements calibrate the whole system; their biases ossify into rubric defaults; latecomers face an in-group that defines correctness. An attacker who is early gets to be the calibration. Conversely, a fair latecomer is structurally underweighted.

Controls:

  1. Recency weighting (early standing does not bank forever). The same decay from Section 6.3 ensures that being early grants a head start, not a permanent throne. Early reviewers must keep being accurate to keep their weight; a latecomer who is consistently accurate catches up, because old correct decisions fade and recent ones dominate.
  1. Onboarding acceleration (compress the climb for the genuinely good). New, accurate contributors and reviewers gain standing faster early in their tenure, a deliberately steeper curve on the first correct decisions, so the in-group's first-mover advantage is bounded. The acceleration is gated on accuracy, so it does not become a Sybil on-ramp (a farm of new accounts still has to be correct to climb).
  1. Periodic recalibration (re-anchor the rubrics). Rubrics, outcome windows, and standing baselines are re-examined on a fixed cadence by the contribution-governance process (Section 6.5), with the explicit goal of detecting and correcting drift introduced by the founding cohort. Recalibration is where a captured-by-the-early-cohort default gets challenged against accumulated outcome data rather than against the founders' priors. [PILOT RESULT: standing distribution over the first cohorts and its evolution after recalibration, to be measured].

Residual risk. Recalibration is itself a governance action and inherits Section 6.5's dependence on the firewall holding. Acceleration trades a small Sybil-acceptance margin for inclusivity; the community sets how steep the early curve is.

6.7 Summary: the cost asymmetry

No single control above is sufficient, and several share a common dependency on personhood (Section 6.1) and on the contribution/token-governance firewall (Section 6.5). That coupling is intentional. The controls are layered so that defeating one attack class tends to force the adversary into another: beating duplicate detection (Section 6.1) requires distinct identities, which raises personhood cost; beating one reviewer (Section 6.2) requires beating the random quorum; beating the quorum requires concentration that Section 6.3 caps; beating the outcome itself (Section 6.4) requires forging signed, on-chain-committed ensemble data (where those signal-integrity controls are deployed; today several are designs).

We restate the goal so a skeptical reader can hold us to it: the system does not promise that gaming is impossible. It promises that, attack class by attack class, the cheapest path to extracting treasury value converges on actually doing the work, and that the pilot (Section 7) is where these cost terms must stop being asserted and start being measured. [PILOT RESULT: leakage, retention, reviewer accuracy, and spam-volume figures from the bug-report pilot loop, to be measured against the evaluation plan in Section 8].

7. Field Grounding and the Pilot

7.1 Field grounding

The mechanism in this paper is not derived from first principles in the abstract. It is motivated by the author's stated multi-year, direct observation of adversarial behaviour inside crypto communities [FIELD DATA: operator to confirm the operating period, the communities, and the scope of the observed history], and by a small pilot deployment that pilots the bug-report contribution path while leaving payout for later. This subsection states what that observation contributes and, equally important, where it does not yet substitute for measurement. The qualitative grounding is treated as design motivation for which failure modes a reward mechanism must survive; only the pilot data in Section 8 can tell us whether the mechanism survives them. We mark every point where real evidence must replace assertion.

What was observed. The operator's stated experience spans community moderation, contributor onboarding, and treasury-style reward decisions across multiple token communities. Four adversarial patterns recurred often enough to be treated as design constraints rather than incidents.

Bought activity. Visible engagement (messages, reactions, votes, referral clicks) is cheap to purchase and was purchased routinely. A reward keyed to activity counts pays the buyer, not the contributor. [FIELD DATA: operator to supply a specific observed episode of paid-engagement farming, e.g. the campaign, the metric inflated, and the approximate fraction of activity judged inauthentic.]

Mercenary participation. A recurring cohort joined for a reward window, extracted value, and left, contributing nothing that outlasted the payout. Retention past the incentive was near zero for this cohort. [FIELD DATA: operator to supply an observed retention drop after a reward program ended, e.g. share of reward-window participants still active N weeks later.]

Sybil and copied-content farming. Single operators ran many identities, and "contributions" were frequently copied, lightly reworded, or template-generated to clear a low activity bar. The cost of fabricating a contribution was far below the reward it captured. [FIELD DATA: operator to supply an observed instance of one actor controlling multiple identities, or of duplicated content claiming separate rewards.]

Capital capturing rewards. Value designed to reward work instead accrued to whoever already held capital (liquidity providers, early holders, large stakers), because the distribution rail was wired to holdings, not to contribution. The humans who moderated, onboarded, and fixed things received a small residual. [FIELD DATA: operator to supply an observed split between capital-side and contributor-side payout in a real program.]

How observation maps to design. Each pattern maps to a specific design commitment, stated earlier in formal terms:

  • Bought activity and copied-content farming motivate outcome anchoring: credit attaches to whether the reported problem was real and the fix held, not to activity volume (Section 3.4).
  • Sybil farming motivates identity-bound, non-transferable credit and standing-weighted review, so that fabricating a contribution requires passing a reviewer who carries reputational cost (Section 3.2 and Section 6.1).
  • Mercenary participation motivates durable, non-expiring credit with windowed outcome validation, so that value tracks contributions that survive their outcome window rather than contributions that merely happened (Section 3.4).
  • Capital capture motivates a treasury-funded, contribution-pro-rata distribution decoupled from holdings (Section 4.1).

The honest limit of this grounding. This is qualitative grounding, not evidence. A multi-year span of pattern recognition is sufficient to justify the design choices and to rule out mechanisms that are obviously gameable. It is not sufficient to claim the mechanism works. The patterns above are reported from memory and operator notes, not from an instrumented dataset, and recollection is itself subject to selection. We therefore make a deliberate separation: the field grounding constrains the design, and the minimal pilot in Section 7.2 is the only source we treat as evidence. Every [FIELD DATA] marker above is a point where the operator must attach a concrete, checkable episode or figure before any reader should accept the corresponding claim as more than motivation.

7.2 Pilot: a bug-report pilot loop

The mechanism described in Sections 3 and 4 is abstract. This subsection shows that it has a minimal bug-report instantiation and specifies the rows needed to test the evaluation hypotheses. We describe the pilot generically: it is a Telegram community bot with a structured bug-report command, a human reviewer who knows the project, a durable contribution record, and a treasury payout that is planned, not yet live. We make no claim that the loop is complete; we claim only that it is the smallest faithful instance of the contribution layer, and that every data-dependency claim remains subject to the pilot-capability markers below.

The loop. The pilot implements one contribution type: a confirmed bug report. The loop has four stages, each mapping to one element of the formal mechanism.

Submission to a durable claim. A user invokes a /ticket command and submits a structured report (a short title and description, optionally an image). The system assigns a monotonic ticket number and a timestamp, and writes a durable row. This is the formal contribution claim $x$ of Section 3.1: a structured, evidence-bearing submission with a stable identity, not a chat message that scrolls away.

Standing-weighted review to an outcome-anchored judgement. A human reviewer who knows the project (an admin/maintainer) evaluates whether the report describes a real defect. The reviewer here carries the standing role of Section 3.2. [PILOT CAPABILITY: operator to confirm reviewer configuration in deployment; the design as run intends standing to be held by a small set of trusted maintainers rather than an open reviewer pool, which is the cold-start configuration discussed in Section 6.6.] The judgement is recorded as a reviewer-confirmation event and a human verdict on the row. Crucially, the question asked is "is this a real bug?", not "is this report popular?", this is the outcome-anchoring of Section 3.4 applied to the bug-report contribution type.

Outcome window to did the fix hold. A bug report is one of the rare soft-to-hard contribution types where the outcome is comparatively observable: a confirmed bug is one whose fix can be checked. The outcome window for this type is "the fix shipped and the defect did not recur within the window." This is the cleanest contribution type to pilot precisely because its outcome is the least gameable, which is also why it is the honest first type and not proof that the mechanism generalises to moderation or onboarding (see Section 5 and the limitations in Section 8.5).

Durable credit to identity-bound, non-transferable, kept by the contributor. On confirmation, the system writes a contribution-credit record tied to the reporter's identity and a receipt the contributor keeps. The credit is non-transferable and does not expire, the durable credit c_i of Section 3.5. A separate proof event is recorded when the credit row is written, so the credit and its justification are linked in the append-only record.

Treasury payout to planned, on test funds first. The distribution step of Section 4.1 is not yet live. The treasury exists in design; the first payouts are planned to run on test funds, pro-rata to each contributor's share of total durable credit, before any real value is distributed. We state this plainly: the pilot is meant to prove the contribution and credit path before it proves the payout path. [PILOT RESULT: to be measured, first test-fund distribution, once executed: total credit, number of recipients, per-recipient share.]

What the pilot records. The pilot is designed to write, per ticket, a row carrying at least: a ticket number, submission timestamp, the reviewer's confirmation event and human verdict, the credit-written event, and close timestamp. These fields are the raw material for the evaluation. The mapping is exact:

  • ticket rows map to submitted claims,
  • reviewer-confirmation events and verdicts map to standing-weighted judgements,
  • credit records map to minted durable credit,
  • the timestamp delta from submission to confirmation maps to time-to-confirm.

If each of these is present as a stored row, every hypothesis in Section 8 becomes computable from data the system produces, not from a survey or a manual count. [PILOT CAPABILITY: operator to confirm which of these row fields are written in the current deployment versus planned.]

8. Evaluation

We do not report results: at the time of writing the pilot has not yet accumulated enough confirmed contributions to draw conclusions, and reporting numbers now would violate the honesty constraint of this paper. Instead we operationalise the four evaluation questions into falsifiable hypotheses, each defined as a quantity intended to be computable from the pilot's rows once the marked row fields are confirmed (Section 7.2), each with a stated falsification condition and a placeholder for the measured value. This converts the evaluation from a wish-list into a pre-registered measurement plan against a pilot system.

Throughout, let $T$ be the set of ticket rows over the measurement window, $C \subseteq T$ the subset with a reviewer confirmation, and $K \subseteq C$ the subset that minted a durable credit record.

8.1 Reward leakage

Construct. Leakage is reward (or, pre-payout, credit) that attaches to a contribution that was not real work. In the pilot, a credit is justified only if the contribution it rewards was confirmed and its outcome held.

Operational quantity. Define credit leakage as the share of minted credit whose underlying contribution was later reversed, i.e. credit rows whose ticket was subsequently judged not-a-real-bug on appeal, or whose "fix" recurred inside the outcome window:

$$ L \;=\; \frac{\bigl|\{\,k \in K : \text{ticket}(k)\text{ reversed within window}\,\}\bigr|}{|K|}. $$

Hypothesis H1 (leakage is bounded). Outcome anchoring is expected to keep $L$ low: $L \le \varepsilon_L$ for a pre-set threshold $\varepsilon_L$. Falsified if a non-trivial fraction of minted credit is reversed, i.e. $L > \varepsilon_L$, which would mean confirmation did not track real outcomes. [PILOT RESULT: to be measured, $L$, with $|K|$ and the reversal count.] [FIELD DATA: operator to set $\varepsilon_L$ from the program's tolerance before measurement, to keep H1 pre-registered.]

8.2 Contributor retention

Construct. A mechanism that pays durable, identity-bound credit for real work should retain contributors past a single reward window, the opposite of the mercenary pattern in Section 7.1.

Operational quantity. For contributors whose first confirmed credit lands in cohort month $m$, define $N$-window retention as the share who submit at least one further confirmed contribution within $N$ windows:

$$ R_N(m) \;=\; \frac{|\{\,u : u \text{ first credited in } m,\ \exists\ \text{later confirmed ticket from } u \le N\}|}{|\{\,u : u \text{ first credited in } m\}|}. $$

Hypothesis H2 (durable credit retains). $R_N$ for credited contributors exceeds the baseline return rate of submitters whose tickets were not confirmed (the comparison isolates the effect of earning durable credit, not merely of showing up). Falsified if credited and non-credited submitters return at indistinguishable rates, i.e. earning credit confers no retention signal. [PILOT RESULT: to be measured, $R_N$ for credited vs non-credited cohorts, with cohort sizes.]

8.3 Reviewer accuracy

Construct. Standing is only meaningful if reviewers who hold it judge correctly over the long run. Accuracy here is agreement between a confirmation and the eventual outcome, not inter-reviewer agreement at decision time.

Operational quantity. For confirmed tickets with a resolved outcome, define reviewer accuracy as the share whose confirmation was vindicated by the outcome window (confirmed-and-fix-held, or rejected-and-no-real-defect):

$$ A \;=\; \frac{|\{\,c \in C : \text{verdict}(c) = \text{outcome}(c)\}|}{|\{\,c \in C : \text{outcome resolved}\}|}. $$

Hypothesis H3 (standing tracks accuracy). Reviewers with higher accumulated standing show higher $A$; equivalently, standing updates driven by outcomes (Section 3.2) move reviewers toward, not away from, ground truth. Falsified if $A$ is uncorrelated with standing, or if high-standing reviewers are no more accurate than new ones, which would mean the standing function rewards the wrong signal. [PILOT RESULT: to be measured, $A$ overall and by standing band, with resolved-outcome counts.] Caveat: with a small trusted-maintainer reviewer configuration (Section 7.2), the standing distribution is narrow; this hypothesis becomes testable only as the reviewer pool widens, and we flag it as underpowered at pilot start.

8.4 Spam volume

Construct. A submission gate that costs nothing invites spam; the mechanism's claim is that outcome-anchored review, not a fee, suppresses low-effort submissions over time because they earn no durable credit.

Operational quantity. Define the spam (non-confirmation) rate as the share of submitted tickets that a reviewer judged not a real defect:

$$ S \;=\; \frac{|T| - |C|}{|T|}, $$

and track its trajectory $S(t)$ across the window per submitting identity.

Hypothesis H4 (spam does not pay, so it declines). Because rejected tickets mint no credit, the per-identity spam rate falls over time as contributors learn the gate, i.e. $S(t)$ is non-increasing for returning identities, and repeat low-effort submitters drop out. Falsified if $S(t)$ is flat or rising, or if the same identities resubmit rejected-type tickets at a stable rate, meaning the lack of payout does not deter them and a harder gate is needed. [PILOT RESULT: to be measured, $S$ overall, $S(t)$ trend, and the repeat-spam-identity count.]

8.5 Scope and limits of this evaluation

Three honest limits bound what the pilot can show.

  1. One easy contribution type. Bug reports were chosen because their outcome is observable. H1 to H4 testing on this type is evidence that the loop runs, not that the crux (Section 5) is solved: soft work like moderation and onboarding has outcomes that are themselves hard to measure, and the pilot does not yet test those.
  2. No payout yet. The treasury distribution is planned on test funds (Section 7.2); until it runs, leakage (H1) is measured in credit, not in distributed value, and any incentive effect of real payout is untested.
  3. Narrow reviewer configuration. Standing is currently held by a small trusted-maintainer set [PILOT CAPABILITY: operator to confirm], so H3 is underpowered until the reviewer pool widens and standing actually varies.

Stating these limits is not a hedge, it is the point. The contribution of this evaluation is a pre-registered, falsifiable plan intended to be wired to a system that must produce the necessary rows before any result claim is promoted, so that the numbers, when they arrive, are measurements and not advocacy.

9. Limitations and Future Work

We restate the limitations the paper has already conceded in context, gather the ones that bear on deployment, and name the future work each one points to.

Not trustless. The mechanism depends on human review and on off-chain outcome signals. There is no point at which a machine alone decides whether soft work held. This is a deliberate choice following Ostrom (the durable governance is human and community-internal), but it means the system inherits every failure mode of human institutions: fatigue, bias, capture. The threat model (Section 6) makes these expensive, not impossible.

The crux is only partly solved. Outcome anchoring is strong exactly where outcomes are observable (bug fixes) and weakest exactly where the paper most needs it (moderation, onboarding, content). For those classes the "outcome" is itself a proxy and is itself gameable (Goodhart's law, Section 5.3). The five defenses in Section 5 and the peer-prediction fallback (Section 6.2 control 6) would make truthful reporting an equilibrium, not a dominant strategy, and the soft classes remain the part of the design most likely to fail under a patient, well-resourced adversary.

Several anti-gaming controls are designs, not deployed. Section 5's hold-out metrics (5.4), rotating rubrics (5.5), outcome ensembles (5.7), and the peer-prediction scoring rule (5.6, and Section 6.2 control 6), together with Section 6.4's signal ensembles, signed outcome attestation, and on-chain outcome commitments, are specified but not yet deployed. The minimal pilot is scoped to the bug-fix class with delayed/retroactive confirmation (5.8) and staked single-reviewer judgement; row-level proof must confirm which parts are active. Sections 5 and 6 describe the intended defense surface; Section 9 is the source of truth on what runs today.

Not strategy-proof. The incentive analysis (Section 3.7) gives the regime where honesty is the better strategy and names where it breaks: cheap fabrication, weak proxies, fresh reviewers with little standing at risk, and captured audits. We claim incentive-alignment within a regime, never incentive-proofness.

Complexity. The mechanism has many parameters (θ_τ, q_τ, W(τ), ω_τ, β_τ, α, λ, γ, audit rate, influence caps). Each is a governance surface and each is a place to get the tuning wrong. A small community may not be able to staff the reviewer pool the controls assume (Section 6.3, Section 6.6). Reducing this parameter surface, and finding defaults that work across community sizes, is open work.

Future work.

  • Widen the reviewer pool and re-run H1 to H4. The single-reviewer pilot cannot test standing dynamics (Section 8.3). The first priority is multi-reviewer operation so the EMA and standing-weighted quorum can do real work; peer-prediction scoring remains a later soft-work backstop to implement and measure.
  • Instrument the soft classes. Move moderation, onboarding, content, and localization from planned to active pilot lanes (Section 5.2), and measure where activity counts and eventual outcomes diverge [FIELD DATA placeholder, Section 5.9].
  • Run the test-fund payout. Close the loop from credit to distributed value on test funds (Section 7.2), then measure leakage in distributed value rather than in credit.
  • Deploy and measure the truthful-elicitation layer. The peer-prediction and Bayesian Truth Serum machinery (Miller, Resnick, Zeckhauser 2005; Prelec 2004) is specified as a future soft-work backstop but not yet deployed; implementing and measuring it is the most direct attack on the crux.
  • Harden outcome-signal provenance. Move from append-only ticket rows to signed, on-chain-committed outcome ensembles (Section 6.4) for treasury-settled outcomes.

10. Conclusion

Crypto reward systems pay for activity because activity is easy to measure, and they pay capital because capital is easy to address. Both are the wrong target. Activity is cheap to fake, so paying for it funds farm-and-dump; capital already captures the upside, so paying it again leaves the humans who do the work with a residual. This paper described a two-layer mechanism that redirects a flat per-activity fee into a community treasury and pays that treasury, pro-rata, to durable, non-transferable, identity-bound credit minted only for work that held up against an outcome, judged by reviewers whose standing rises and falls with the long-run correctness of their calls.

None of the parts are new. Ostrom gives the governance shell, EigenTrust the reputation substrate, Douceur the reason to bind reward to work rather than to identity, peer prediction and the Bayesian Truth Serum the truthful-elicitation backbone for soft work, retroactive public-goods funding the payout timing, and Goodhart's law the problem statement. The contribution is the integration into a single loop, an honest semi-formal model that states the regime where the incentives hold and the regimes where they do not, and a field-grounded design hypothesis from the author's stated years of operating adversarial communities plus a minimal pilot.

We are deliberately unfinished. The crux, measuring outcomes for soft work without creating a new target, is only partly solved, and we say so. The pilot targets one easy contribution type, has no live payout, and a narrow reviewer configuration. Several of the anti-gaming controls described in Sections 5 and 6 are designs not yet deployed, which Section 9 states plainly. What the pilot must demonstrate is a loop that writes the rows, so the evaluation in Section 8 is a pre-registered, falsifiable plan rather than a promise. When the numbers arrive, they will be measurements. Until then, this is integration of known primitives, carefully wired and honestly bounded, not a new theory.
