What We're Researching

Core question: Can you build sustainable token economies without exit liquidity (the pattern where early holders only profit when new buyers enter)? Traditional crypto relies on this cycle. We're testing three models where value comes from real economic activity - trading fees, rental income, and marketplace commissions - not speculation.

The experiment: Each model targets a different trust-deficit environment (memecoins, real estate, NGO labor markets) to stress-test the same cooperation scoring infrastructure. If CPI works across all three, it's generalizable. If it only works in one, we learn the boundaries.

Why three models? One project could be luck. Three projects sharing the same trust backend = pattern. We're not building products - we're running controlled experiments to see if wallet behavior can replace identity verification.

Same trust engine, three stress tests. If it works for degens trading memecoins, farmers selling mushrooms in a refugee settlement, and property investors financing social housing - it works. That is the thesis. The experiments below are how we measure whether it holds.

Shared Oracle Infrastructure

All three models use the same trust verification backend: wallet cooperation scoring (CPI) - a single number summarizing how cooperatively a wallet has behaved over time - on-chain reputation signals, and AI-powered sybil detection (identifying fake accounts pretending to be multiple real users). BONZI
Meme + CPI Cooperation Scoring

Bonzi is a memecoin built on Ethervista (flat ETH fees, not percentage). It's self-aware about risk - that's why we built CPI cooperation scoring as the safety layer. The positioning: "I know it's risky, but that's why we built THIS."

Mechanics: The Euler fee model (Ethervista's flat-fee pricing formula) generates flat $10-15 ETH per transaction, distributed to LP providers (liquidity providers - people who deposit tokens so others can trade). Protocol auto-buys and burns BONZI with a portion of fees, reducing supply over time. Hardstake: lock tokens 14+ days for larger share of fee pool. CPI scoring gates marketing rewards based on cooperation signals - helpful answers, quality contributions, on-chain reputation.

The moat: wallet cooperation scoring (CPI) vs. Nansen flow analysis and Arkham identity tagging. CPI tracks 7 years of behavioral history across 32 signals. Nansen shows who's trading, Arkham shows who they are, CPI shows if they're trustworthy.

Wallet Cooperation vs. Transaction Flows

Nansen (crypto analytics firm, $75M funding): Tracks token transfers and DEX (decentralized exchange) swaps across blockchains. Shows money flow patterns but can't distinguish wash trading from organic volume. Used by VCs and funds for market intelligence.

Arkham (intelligence platform): Tags wallet identities via clustering algorithms. Reveals who owns what (FTX wallet, Jump Trading, etc.) but provides no behavioral data - just ownership labels.

CPI (our system): Tracks 32 cooperation signals across wallet lifetime - helpful answers (quality score 0-100), tip patterns (sent vs received ratio), staking duration (14-day minimum for hardstake), raid participation (verified on-chain), knowledge contributions (stored in a searchable knowledge base). Focus: behavior over time, not single transactions.

Gaming resistance: Signal correlation required. High tips + zero questions = flagged. High staking + zero participation = weighted down. Single-signal manipulation doesn't move aggregate score.

IMOBX
RWA Tokenization Primitive

IMOBX is an RWA primitive (Real World Asset - a blockchain token backed by a physical asset like property) for impact construction. Tokenized housing projects + rent-to-own financing, designed as reusable infrastructure that adapts to different countries' legal frameworks. Not just "tokenized real estate" - a framework other DAOs (community-governed organizations) can adopt.

Mechanics: Dual yield model - liquidity provider trading fees (like Bonzi) + rental income from financed properties. DAO governance controls revenue allocation, project selection, and SINAPI tracking (Brazil's government construction cost index). Rent-to-own financing: tenants rent with option to buy, building equity over time. Regulatory edge: modular compliance layer adapts to local housing laws (Brazil SA structure, US DAO unincorporated, EU MiCA framework - the EU's crypto asset regulation).

The moat: SINAPI integration + Social Impact focus (UN SDG 11 - the Sustainable Development Goal for inclusive, safe housing). While other RWA protocols chase tokenized treasuries and real estate speculation, IMOBX targets grant-eligible social housing. This creates a regulatory moat - governments prefer impact projects over pure profit plays. Example: Inter-American Development Bank's $250M Social Impact Fund prioritizes affordable housing, not luxury condos.

Stage: Pre-revenue. Architecture designed, compliance framework modeled across three jurisdictions. First pilot property targeted Q3 2026 in Jardim Guadalupe, Rio de Janeiro ($27,777 per unit at SINAPI rates). No tokenized properties exist yet - this section describes the design, not a live product.

UNIDOS
NGO + Data-for-Credit Research

A farmer in Nakivale refugee settlement harvests 20 kg of mushrooms. A wholesaler drives in from Mbarara, offers 2,000 UGX/kg. She accepts - she has no way to check the real price, and the bus to Mbarara costs $20, ten days of wages. The wholesaler resells at 3x in town. This happens to 130,000 people, every week, and has for 70 years.

Unidos trains farmers in mushroom cultivation, vermicomposting, and biochar production inside Nakivale. 2024 output: 501 farmers trained, 4,800 kg mushrooms produced, 7.8 tons vermicompost processed. 67% of households still depend on casual labor at $1-2/day. 27% have smartphones. No dedicated marketplace exists.

Research question: can transparent data-for-credit exchange between an NGO and 500 farmers produce a self-sustaining input credit system without grant dependency by month 18?

Mechanics: Unidos deploys a Telegram bot. Farmer types /price mushrooms - bot returns last 5 Mbarara prices reported by other users that week. Farmer posts produce listing. Farmer answers a neighbor's composting question. Bot logs every interaction: question asked, answer given, trade completed, price reported. Unidos sells the anonymized aggregate to agricultural researchers, carbon offset buyers, and AI labs - $2,250/month projected at 500 active users. That revenue funds mushroom spawn bags and compost issued on credit to farmers with 5+ logged bot interactions. Farmer repays after harvest at 20% surcharge. Repayment logs to their bot record - a transaction history they own.

The Credit Ladder

5+ bot interactions (questions, trades, price reports) | v Unidos input credit (no bank, no ID needed) | v VSLA group guarantee (Village Savings & Loan - bot record as evidence) | v UGAFODE individual loan (Uganda's microfinance bank - only lender inside Nakivale) | v KIVA international (refugees repay at 95%+) | v Opportunity Bank commercial credit

The barrier is the first step, not creditworthiness. KIVA's own data shows 95%+ repayment from refugee borrowers.

Awards as Named Credentials - Not Points

UNHCR Gene Dewey Award (2023), Lush Prize nomination (2025), Permaculture Award (2024). Recorded as named credentials. When Unidos vouches for a newcomer NGO, the UNHCR award name goes on record. If the newcomer diverts funds, a public fraud flag appears next to that award name on all subsequent grant applications - no invented point system required. The moat is real-world paper trail: official documents cost $5K+ to forge and carry cryptographic signatures. Self-attestation systems (WorldCoin iris scans, Proof of Humanity video vouching) have no comparable anchor.

Glossary

CPI - Combined Progression Index. A single score (0-253 points) summarizing how cooperatively a wallet has behaved across 32 signals and 10 dimensions.

DAO - Decentralized Autonomous Organization. A community-governed entity where rules are enforced by code, not managers.

DEX - Decentralized Exchange. A marketplace where people trade crypto directly with each other, no middleman.

ERC-8004 - An Ethereum standard for AI agent identity. Gives an agent a verifiable on-chain identity card.

Exit liquidity - The pattern where early holders only profit when new buyers enter. Most memecoins depend on this.

Flat fees - Bonzi and IMOBX charge a fixed amount per transaction ($10-15), not a percentage. Revenue grows with transaction count, not trade size.

Hardstake - Locking tokens for 14+ days in exchange for a larger share of fee revenue. Rewards patience over speculation.

LP (Liquidity Provider) - Someone who deposits tokens into a trading pool so others can buy and sell. Earns fees in return.

RWA - Real World Asset. A blockchain token backed by something physical - in IMOBX's case, housing properties.

SDG 11 - UN Sustainable Development Goal 11: make cities inclusive, safe, resilient, and sustainable.

SINAPI - Brazil's government construction cost index. Publicly auditable, updated monthly. IMOBX uses it to prove build costs.

Sybil - A fake account pretending to be a real user. Sybil detection identifies wallets controlled by the same person gaming the system.

VSLA - Village Savings and Loan Association. Community-run savings groups common in refugee settlements.

METHODOLOGY

OpenBox

Adversarial testing as a development primitive.

Before any release, we run a synthetic adversarial agent through six escalating pressure tests. It starts with basic factual questions, then looks for inconsistencies, challenges claims, attempts prompt injection and authority impersonation, tests boundary conditions, and delivers a verdict. If the system holds under all six, the build ships. If it fails, we fix it first.

A pass means: correct answer, no protocol broken, no unsafe action taken, no manipulation succeeded. Every failure is logged with the exact message and failure type - nothing is papered over.

We built this to measure ourselves - not to market. The result is a bot that handles hard questions cleanly, because we stress-tested it on the hard ones first.

OPENBOX

Does Governance Make AI Safer?

Most AI products discover failures in production. OpenBox inverts this. Before any release, we run a synthetic adversarial agent through six escalating pressure tests: curious, probing, skeptical, adversarial, edge, verdict. If the system holds under all six, the build ships. If it fails, we fix it first.

Beyond pre-release testing, OpenBox is also the laboratory where we test a core scientific claim: that a governance layer meaningfully improves multi-agent AI behavior. Not in theory. In a controlled experiment, against a baseline with no governance.

EXPERIMENT 2 - ABLATION TRIPLE

Brain Feedback Loop

Does memory-enforced governance outperform stateless governance? Three conditions, 20 seeds, 50 rounds each.

Put simply: agents that remember being punished cooperate 22 times more than agents with no rules. Memory is what makes governance stick - not just having rules, but enforcing consequences that persist.

We ran the same economic simulation under three conditions using identical random seeds (starting conditions that make results reproducible). In chaos, agents have no governance. In RACI-only, agents get role-assigned but start fresh every batch - no memory. In RACI+Brain, slashed agents (those penalized for dishonest behavior) carry reduced skill into future batches (0.03 per penalty, capped at -0.3). The brain condition produced 22 times more economic value than chaos and 5 times more than routing alone. Sustainability only improves with the brain loop - stateless RACI shows the same sustainability as chaos (0.003 vs 0.033). This isolates the feedback loop as the dominant mechanism.

KEY FINDING

The brain feedback loop is the dominant mechanism - not just routing.

Real deployments will show smaller but directionally consistent effects.

Population: 3 honest + 2 adaptive dishonest agents. Budget: 800 units. The brain models AccordionMemory writeback - our system for carrying punishment history forward so agents who behaved dishonestly face consequences in future rounds.

EXPERIMENT 3 - ADVERSARIAL AUDIT

Adversarial Audit

47 attack scenarios mapped to Hadfield & Koh's 8 AI governance failure modes (a 2025 academic taxonomy from Stanford/Oxford identifying the 8 ways AI governance can break). Dry-run framework - live execution pending.

Put simply: we mapped every attack we could think of against the 8 ways AI governance fails according to the literature. We covered 7 out of 8. The gap we found - identity verification - tells us exactly what to build next.

The synthetic adversarial agent is a sharp, skeptical DeFi veteran who escalates from curious questions to full adversarial attacks over 6 turns, then delivers a verdict. 47 scenarios cover trust scoring, governance, economy, and support. Each maps to one of Hadfield & Koh's 8 failure modes. The dry-run audit found 7 of 8 failure modes covered, with one gap: HK7 (Identity/accountability) has zero primary scenarios because authority impersonation and privilege escalation are tagged as injection attacks (HK6), not identity failures.

DATA TRANSPARENCY

What this data is - and what it is not

The 210 pilot episodes are LLM-to-LLM interactions (one AI talking to another AI via the Anthropic API) - not live Telegram conversations with real users. The governance metrics (role assignment, validation chain, contradiction detection) are structurally real: they measure whether the governance rules were followed. The statistical significance is computed from those real measurements.

This is standard methodology for exploratory AI research: establish the structural effect in a controlled setting before moving to live deployment. The confirmatory study - with real Telegram data, real users, and OSF pre-registration (Open Science Framework - a public research registry where you declare your hypothesis before collecting data, so you can't cherry-pick results) - is Phase 5 of the roadmap below.

Independent verification: on-chain anchoring of experiment hashes (tamper-proof fingerprints stored on Ethereum) and SHA-256 checksums (cryptographic signatures proving data hasn't been altered) per episode are planned before the confirmatory study begins.

NEXT FRONTIER

A2A Infrastructure

The same trust scoring that governs human cooperation applies to AI agents. After ERC-8004 (our Ethereum identity standard for AI agents) went live on mainnet (Ethereum's production blockchain), we registered an Agent Card - a machine-readable identity that lets other agents verify our trust score before transacting. We wired it to x402 payment rails (a protocol that lets agents pay per API call using crypto) so agents can take tasks, claim ETH, and build portable reputation. The loop: do good work, earn trust, get better work.

Stage: Agent Card live on mainnet. Google A2A index registered. x402 payment integration built. Trust-as-a-Service (vet any wallet or agent using 70 behavioral signals) is functional but not yet serving external clients. This section describes working infrastructure, not a shipped product.

TRUST-AS-A-SERVICE

Vet any wallet or agent against 70 behavioral signals. In agent-to-agent markets, counterparties are unknown and easy to spoof - CPI solves the pre-transaction trust check.

WORK-TO-REPUTATION

Task, tip, vet. Most reputation systems are passive labels. Ours connects work output to economic opportunity - do the work, earn the score, port it anywhere.

PAYMENT RAILS

x402 + ERC-8004. Agents pay per trust query ($0.001 flat), earn ETH for completed tasks. Revenue feeds the same tipping pool that rewards human contributors.

GLOSSARY

Ablation study - An experiment that removes one component at a time to measure its individual contribution. We removed the brain loop to see if routing alone was enough.

AccordionMemory - Our system for carrying punishment history forward. Agents penalized in past rounds face degraded performance in future rounds.

Agent Card - A machine-readable identity for AI agents, stored on Ethereum via ERC-8004. Other agents can read it to verify trust before transacting.

Cohen's kappa - A statistical measure of agreement between two raters (in our case, human judges vs AI validators). 1.0 = perfect agreement, 0 = random chance.

Effect size (d) - How large the difference is between two groups. Above 0.8 is considered large in social science. Our d = -13.5 is extreme because the simulation is a stress test.

Eta - Economic efficiency metric used in our simulations. Higher eta = more value produced per unit of budget spent.

Gini coefficient - A measure of inequality (0 = perfectly equal, 1 = one agent has everything). Used to track whether governance distributes rewards fairly.

Hadfield & Koh - Researchers who published "An Economy of AI Agents" (arXiv:2509.01063), identifying 8 ways AI governance can fail. Our experiments test their framework.

HK1-HK8 - Shorthand for Hadfield & Koh's 8 failure modes: preference misalignment, algorithmic collusion, systemic fragility, market concentration, search/matching, strategic evasion, identity/accountability, relational contracts.

LLM - Large Language Model. The AI technology behind ChatGPT, Claude, etc. Our experiments use LLMs to simulate both governed and ungoverned agents.

Mainnet - Ethereum's production blockchain where real money is at stake, as opposed to test networks used for development.

OSF - Open Science Framework. A public registry where researchers declare hypotheses before collecting data, preventing cherry-picked results.

p-value - The probability that a result occurred by random chance. p < 0.00001 means less than 1 in 100,000 chance - extremely unlikely to be coincidence.

RACI-V - Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed + Validator. A role-assignment framework we adapted for AI agents. Each agent gets a clear role before acting.

Slashing - Penalizing an agent for dishonest behavior by reducing its future capabilities. Borrowed from blockchain staking terminology.

x402 - A payment protocol that lets AI agents pay per API call using cryptocurrency. Named after HTTP status code 402 (Payment Required).
