What this is

Bonzi is trying to fix a simple problem: in most online communities, the people who actually help are invisible.

Someone finds bugs, answers questions, calms people down, brings new members in, or keeps the group alive for months. But later, when rewards come, the money usually goes to the biggest holders, the loudest posters, or the people who know how to farm attention.

Bonzi says: keep a real record of useful help. If you do something useful, a trusted person checks it. If it was real, it becomes part of your record. Not a vibe. Not "the founder remembers you." A record on a ledger, not in someone's head.

That record can build over time. It shows that you helped before, that your judgment was good, and that you are someone the community can trust. The ledger does not forget. It does not get rewritten when the founder changes his mind.

The bigger idea is that AI is making everything faster. Companies and bots will judge people by data. If normal people do not have a way to prove their real work, they get left behind.

Bonzi keeps a record of real human work that machines can read. You prove what you did without handing your whole life to a company.

This closed alpha is the first small test of that idea.

You hold the token, so you get in early. This page shows what you can try now and how to help.

An alpha is the first working version of a product. Closed means only a small group gets in before it opens to everyone. You are in that group.

Some parts work. Some parts are rough. Your job is simple: try it, and tell us what breaks.

Why we do it this way

Most projects work the other way around. They make big promises, raise money, and only later try to build something real. Often it never comes.

We did it in reverse. We spent the past year building, quietly, and the first piece works today. You get to use it before we make any noise about it.

We would rather hand you something real than promise you something later.

Why we already know this is needed

Good things get built around real people. You learn what they need by spending time with them and watching closely, not by guessing in a meeting. Most projects skip that part. It is slow, and it does not look impressive.

We spent seven years on it. Running real online communities by hand, every day. Watching what helped people and what let them down.

So we are not guessing about the need. We saw it, up close, for years. The slow part is the strength. You cannot design something like this from a whiteboard.

What is broken, and what we are fixing

In most of crypto, the money goes to whoever already has the most. The biggest holders put cash in, take the rewards, and leave. If you are not one of them, you did the work and got scraps.

The token's money side has already been fixed: steady fees instead of a slow bleed, so the token can hold its value. That was the first step. But the rewards still flow to the money, not to the people doing the work.

That is the part we are building. A fair way to reward the people who actually help and actually stay. Your work was seen, judged honestly, and recorded, with no institution in the middle. Pay the builders and they keep building, and it grows. Reward only the money and the money drains it and leaves.

We are building this for the people who stick around after the hype is gone. The ones who actually know what is broken, and who have a nose for what is real.

The first step of that fix, bug reporting, is live today, and you can try it. The lane is called Bug & Earn.

Bug & Earn, the first lane

The first way to help, and the first thing you can try today, is called Bug & Earn. You find something broken, you report it, and if it turns out to be real, that work goes on your record.

We start with bugs for a plain reason: a bug is something we can actually check. A like can be bought. Posts and clicks can be farmed by people who do not care about the project. But a bug is either real or it is not, and a fix either holds or it does not. That makes it the cleanest first test of whether useful work can be recorded fairly.

Here is the path from report to record. The tags show what works today, what is being checked with real users, and what is not live yet.

1

You report a bug. Live now
Open /ticket in the bot. Write what you did, what you expected, and what went wrong. A screenshot or link helps. You get a number and a time back. This works today. Try it.

2

It becomes a case. Checking now
If other people report the same bug, the reports are grouped so one fix covers everyone. This grouping is being proven with the first reports now.

3

A person checks it. Checking now
Someone who knows the project reads it and decides if it is a real bug. A person, not a program. What matters is whether it was really a bug, not how loud you were.

4

You get a record. Checking now
Your credit is written down, and a receipt closes it out: your number, the decision, and what happened. This step goes live only after it works for real reporters.

Step one works today. The rest of the path is built and being checked with the first people. Treasury payout is not live. The status section below says exactly where each part stands.

What you earn, and what it builds

When a person confirms your bug is real, you earn THANKS. This credit step is in checking now, not yet live. THANKS is your task credit: a plain record that you did something useful and a person checked it, not just that you claimed it.

THANKS feeds into your ZEAL. ZEAL is your trust score, the way the community reads how useful and reliable you have been over time. It grows slowly, from real work that someone checked. You cannot buy it, sell it, trade it, or top it up with money.

A badge or a high score does not, by itself, pay you, make you an admin, or give you a vote. It is a record of what you have done, nothing more and nothing less. Early contributor badges mark reviewed participation; they do not create authority.

Money buys tokens. It does not buy trust. Trust is earned by work a person checked, and that is what we are recording here.

What is live, what we are checking, what comes later

Plain status. We only call something live when it leaves a record you can check yourself.

Live now: the /ticket intake. Open /ticket in the bot and send a report. It comes back with a number and a 48h target. Test it today, this minute, without taking our word for it.

Checking now, with the first real users: receipt delivery and contribution credit. The software is built, but we only call this live when a real bug is confirmed by an admin, the reporter gets credit, and the receipt lands in a record the user can check.

Later: THANKS converting to ETH. That payout rail starts on test ETH, practice money on a test network with no real value. No real-value payout is live today, and no payout result is claimed here.

Not running at all yet: there is no honesty penalty, no locked deposit, no voting weight, and no token reward live today. None of it. When any of those start, this page will say so in these words. If a page tells you they are running before you can point to the record, close the page.

What comes next

Bugs are the first lane, not the only one. Once this loop runs cleanly with real people, we open more ways to help that can be checked the same way: good questions, useful answers, helping new people find their footing. Each one reviewed by a person. Each one adding to your record.

The payout side comes last, on purpose. When it starts, it starts on test funds with no real value, so the whole path is proven before a cent moves. We would rather be slow and right than fast and sorry.

The plan in one line: prove one honest report-to-record loop with checkable records, show the evidence, then open the next.

How to try it

Open the bot. Send /intro to get in, then /ticket when you find a bug. (/intro lets you in, /ticket sends the report.) You get a number back right away.

When a person confirms your bug, you get your record. One confirmed ticket is the whole idea made real: your work was seen, judged honestly, and recorded. Not in someone's head. On a ledger. Then tell us straight: did that feel like something real, or not? A no helps us more than a polite yes. If anything on this page sounds like a sales pitch, tell us that too. That is a bug as well.

We are looking for people who notice how things work, and who have seen what is broken in crypto and want a fairer version.

Try the live part. Open the bot, send /intro, then /ticket when you find a bug.
