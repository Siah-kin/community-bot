Sustainable Crypto Communities: The Short Version

A five-minute read. The full paper, with the formal model and references, is linked at the end.

The problem

Most crypto projects reward two things: activity and money.

Likes, posts, and daily messages get rewarded. So do the people who already hold the most tokens. The people who do the real work, fixing bugs, helping newcomers, moderating chats, keeping the project alive, usually get nothing that lasts.

So communities fill up with people chasing the quick reward, who leave the moment it stops paying. The ones who actually build get scraps.

What we propose

Reward the work, not the noise and not the money. The shape is simple:

  • You do something useful and report it, with proof.
  • A real person who knows the project checks whether it actually helped, judged on the result (did the bug get fixed, did the problem go away), not on how loud or popular it was.
  • If it holds up, you get a record of it. The record is yours and it does not expire.
  • A shared pot, funded by steady fees on the token, pays out over time, in proportion to the record you have built.

This sends value to the people who do the work, the same way trading fees currently send value to the people with money.

Why we think it can work

None of the parts are new.

Reputation scoring, judging by outcomes, paying for a public good after its value shows, durable records of contribution: these all exist and have been studied for years.

What is new is wiring them together for the messy, adversarial reality of crypto communities, and grounding the design in years of actually running those communities by hand.

The honest part

We are not going to oversell this.

  • The hardest problem is measuring "did it actually help" for soft work like moderation and content. We have a plan for it, not a solved problem. We say so in the paper.
  • Most of the system is built. The reward payout is not switched on yet, and when it starts it starts on test funds with no real value.
  • A real person checking the work means some trust is involved. We reduce it with multiple reviewers, accountability, and audits. We do not pretend it is fully automatic or trustless.

The live test

The first working piece is a bug-report loop inside a Telegram bot.

You report a bug. A person confirms it. You get a record. That is the closed alpha you may already be in. It is also the pilot that will produce the real numbers the full paper is waiting on.
