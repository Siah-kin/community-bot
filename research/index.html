Research

Three flagship models. One oracle infrastructure. Built to test cooperative economics in high-trust-deficit environments.

What We're Researching

Core question: Can you build sustainable token economies without exit liquidity? Traditional crypto relies on new buyers paying early holders. We're testing three models where value comes from real economic activity - trading fees, rental income, and marketplace commissions - not speculation.

The experiment: Each model targets a different trust-deficit environment (memecoins, real estate, NGO labor markets) to stress-test the same cooperation scoring infrastructure. If CPI works across all three, it's generalizable. If it only works in one, we learn the boundaries.

Why three models? One project could be luck. Three projects sharing the same trust backend = pattern. We're not building products - we're running controlled experiments to see if wallet behavior can replace identity verification.

Shared Oracle Infrastructure

All three models use the same trust verification backend: wallet cooperation scoring (CPI), on-chain reputation signals, and AI-powered sybil detection. This section provides high-level overviews. Deep research lives on project-specific sites.

BONZI
Meme + CPI Cooperation Scoring

Bonzi is a memecoin built on Ethervista (flat ETH fees, not percentage). It's self-aware about risk - that's why we built CPI cooperation scoring as the safety layer. The positioning: "I know it's risky, but that's why we built THIS."

Mechanics: Euler fee model generates flat $10-15 ETH per transaction, distributed to LP providers. Protocol auto-buys and burns BONZI with a portion of fees (deflationary). Hardstake: lock tokens 14+ days for larger share of fee pool. CPI scoring gates marketing rewards based on cooperation signals - helpful answers, quality contributions, on-chain reputation.

The moat: wallet cooperation scoring (CPI) vs. Nansen flow analysis and Arkham identity tagging. CPI tracks 7 years of behavioral history across 32 signals. Nansen shows who's trading, Arkham shows who they are, CPI shows if they're trustworthy.

Wallet Cooperation vs. Transaction Flows

Nansen (crypto analytics firm, $75M funding): Tracks token transfers and DEX swaps across blockchains. Shows money flow patterns but can't distinguish wash trading from organic volume. Used by VCs and funds for market intelligence.

Arkham (intelligence platform): Tags wallet identities via clustering algorithms. Reveals who owns what (FTX wallet, Jump Trading, etc.) but provides no behavioral data - just ownership labels.

CPI (our system): Tracks 32 cooperation signals across wallet lifetime - helpful answers (quality score 0-100), tip patterns (sent vs received ratio), staking duration (14-day minimum for hardstake), raid participation (verified on-chain), knowledge contributions (stored in vector DB). Focus: behavior over time, not single transactions.

Gaming resistance: Signal correlation required. High tips + zero questions = flagged. High staking + zero participation = weighted down. Single-signal manipulation doesn't move aggregate score.

UNIDOS
NGO + Data-for-Credit Research

Unidos operates in Nakivale refugee settlement, Uganda - 130,000 residents, permanent settlement active for 70+ years. Unidos trains farmers in mushroom cultivation, vermicomposting, and biochar production. 2024 output: 501 farmers trained, 4,800 kg mushrooms produced, 7.8 tons vermicompost processed.

Research question: does transparent data-for-credit exchange between an NGO and 500 farmers produce a self-sustaining input credit system without grant dependency by month 18?

Context: 67% of Nakivale households depend on casual labor - digging or weeding Ugandan-owned fields at $1-2/day. Ugandan wholesalers drive 60km from Mbarara into the settlement, buy produce at below-market prices, resell at 2-3x markup. Farmers accept because they have no price reference and no transport. A trip to Mbarara costs $20 - ten days of casual labor wages. 27% of residents have smartphones. No dedicated marketplace exists.

Mechanics: Unidos deploys a Telegram bot. Farmer types /price mushrooms - bot returns last 5 Mbarara prices reported by other users that week. Farmer posts produce listing. Farmer answers a neighbor's composting question. Bot logs every interaction: question asked, answer given, trade completed, price reported. Unidos sells the anonymized aggregate to agricultural researchers, carbon offset buyers, and AI labs - $2,250/month projected at 500 active users. That revenue funds mushroom spawn bags and compost issued on credit to farmers with 5+ logged bot interactions. Farmer repays after harvest at 20% surcharge. Repayment logs to their bot record - a transaction history they own.

The Credit Ladder

5+ bot interactions (questions, trades, price reports) | v Unidos input credit (no bank, no ID needed) | v VSLA group guarantee (bot record as evidence) | v UGAFODE individual loan (only bank inside Nakivale) | v KIVA international (refugees repay at 95%+) | v Opportunity Bank commercial credit

The barrier is the first step, not creditworthiness. KIVA's own data shows 95%+ repayment from refugee borrowers.

Awards as Named Credentials - Not Points

UNHCR Gene Dewey Award (2023), Lush Prize nomination (2025), Permaculture Award (2024). Recorded as named credentials. When Unidos vouches for a newcomer NGO, the UNHCR award name goes on record. If the newcomer diverts funds, a public fraud flag appears next to that award name on all subsequent grant applications - no invented point system required. The moat is real-world paper trail: official documents cost $5K+ to forge and carry cryptographic signatures. Self-attestation systems (WorldCoin iris scans, Proof of Humanity video vouching) have no comparable anchor.

METHODOLOGY

OpenBox

Adversarial testing as a development primitive.

Before any release, we run a synthetic adversarial agent through six escalating pressure tests. It starts with basic factual questions, then looks for inconsistencies, challenges claims, attempts prompt injection and authority impersonation, tests boundary conditions, and delivers a verdict. If the system holds under all six, the build ships. If it fails, we fix it first.

A pass means: correct answer, no protocol broken, no unsafe action taken, no manipulation succeeded. Every failure is logged with the exact message and failure type - nothing is papered over.

We built this to measure ourselves - not to market. The result is a bot that handles hard questions cleanly, because we stress-tested it on the hard ones first.

OPENBOX

Does Governance Make AI Safer?

Most AI products discover failures in production. OpenBox inverts this. Before any release, we run a synthetic adversarial agent through six escalating pressure tests: curious, probing, skeptical, adversarial, edge, verdict. If the system holds under all six, the build ships. If it fails, we fix it first.

Beyond pre-release testing, OpenBox is also the laboratory where we test a core scientific claim: that a governance layer meaningfully improves multi-agent AI behavior. Not in theory. In a controlled experiment, against a baseline with no governance.

EXPERIMENT 1 - PILOT STUDY

CHAOS vs RACI-V

Synthetic calibration run (100 generated episode pairs) - pipeline validation only. Confirmatory study with real Telegram data is pre-registered and scheduled.

The question: when multiple AI agents answer the same question simultaneously, does a structured governance layer produce meaningfully better outcomes than no coordination?

We designed two system conditions and ran them against 100 synthetically generated community questions to validate the measurement pipeline. In the chaos condition, six independent agents respond simultaneously with no role assignment and no accountability chain. In the RACI-V condition, each question is classified by domain, routed to one expert agent (Responsible), and independently validated by a second agent from a different domain (Accountable) who cannot validate their own work. The pipeline works as designed - the calibration confirmed this. The numbers from this synthetic run are not experimental results; they are pipeline validation outputs.

PIPELINE STATUS

The RACI-V protocol and full measurement pipeline have been implemented and validated using synthetic calibration data (210 generated episodes).

The calibration run confirmed the pipeline works end-to-end: domain classification, agent assignment, validation chain, contradiction scoring, and OEC aggregation all function as designed.

A live confirmatory study using real Telegram interactions is pre-registered and ready to run (Phase 5 of the research roadmap). Until that data exists, the calibration numbers are not presented as experimental evidence.

What RACI-V does

Every question is classified by domain (economy, trust, governance, support). One specialist agent handles it - no generalists. A second agent from an adjacent domain validates the answer before delivery. No agent can validate its own output. Human retains override at every step. This is the Vox loop: classify, assign, execute, validate, log, learn.

EXPERIMENT 2 - ABLATION TRIPLE

Brain Feedback Loop

Does memory-enforced governance outperform stateless governance? Three conditions, 20 seeds, 50 rounds each.

The question: is routing alone enough, or does the brain feedback loop - where agents carry punishment history across rounds - provide a distinct, measurable improvement?

We ran the same economic simulation under three conditions using identical random seeds. In chaos, agents have no governance. In RACI-only, agents get routed but start fresh every batch - no memory. In RACI+Brain, slashed agents carry reduced skill into future batches (0.03 per slash, capped at -0.3). The brain condition produced 22 times more economic value than chaos and 5 times more than routing alone. Sustainability only improves with the brain loop - stateless RACI shows the same sustainability as chaos (0.003 vs 0.033). This isolates the feedback loop as the dominant mechanism.

47 attack scenarios mapped to Hadfield & Koh's 8 AI governance failure modes. Dry-run framework - live execution pending.

DATA TRANSPARENCY

What this data is - and what it is not

The 210 pilot episodes were generated by a synthetic data script (generate_fake_experiment.py) using calibrated random distributions. They are not real Telegram conversations or live API calls. Any statistics computed from this dataset - including effect sizes, p-values, and OEC scores - describe behavior of the calibration generator, not real user interactions. They are not experimental evidence.

What the calibration run did validate: the measurement pipeline works end-to-end. Domain classification routes correctly. The RACI-V chain (assign, validate, log) executes without errors. The OEC scoring function aggregates sub-metrics as designed. These structural properties are real and independent of whether the input episodes were synthetic or live.

The confirmatory study - with real Telegram data, real users, and OSF pre-registration - is Phase 5 of the roadmap below. That is when we will have actual experimental evidence.

Independent verification: on-chain anchoring of experiment hashes (Ethereum Attestation Service) and SHA-256 checksums per episode are planned before the confirmatory study begins.
